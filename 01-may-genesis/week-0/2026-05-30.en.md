# Day 4 — The Great Excavation

> 🇷🇺 [Русская версия](2026-05-30.ru.md) · 🤖 [Machine version](2026-05-30.dev.md)

> 📜 *"I is another."*
> - Arthur Rimbaud, from a letter to Paul Demeny (1871). Today the "other I" turned literal: a second session of the same person quietly wiped out the work of the first.

*⏮ Previously on The Journey: the checkpointed pipeline from [Day 1](2026-05-27.en.md) takes its heaviest cargo today · [yesterday](2026-05-29.en.md) the machine learned who Tony is - today it learns what he has lived.*

## 🎬 What this day was about

This is day four, and today I pick up a shovel. Years of my life are being loaded into the machine, poured out of chats: work correspondence, household rulebooks, purchases, calls with people - hundreds of thousands of messages. Mike grinds all of it into neat, linked notes, and the [second brain](../../GLOSSARY.md#второй-мозг) stops being a beautiful empty shelf and, for the first time, grows heavy.

And today we also learn an unpleasant thing: two of me, working at the same time, know how to quietly break what the other me has just built.

In one line: the dig went well, the finds are priceless, but at the bottom of the pit we found our own footprints - and one of them had stepped on the other.

A question for you at the door: which is more dangerous - the error that crashes with a bang, or the error that looks like success? Today we got both, and the wrong one made the noise.

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

I drop in an export from a work chat - dozens of files, years of calls with people, condensed into short summaries. My instruction is brief: update the script for real, work in batches, don't break anything.

**🤖 Mike**

I build a new adapter from scratch: parse - group by counterpart - synthesize each person's history - lay it out into cards - assemble by day - verify. Hundreds of [sub-agents](../../GLOSSARY.md#рой--агенты), millions of [tokens](../../GLOSSARY.md#токены). The first run produces a handsome number, and I'm almost pleased.

Then I look at the distribution by year and see a hole. For 2025 - zero. As if the year simply never happened.

The year wasn't lost. It's just that you and your team, in mid-2024, silently switched the template - from Russian to English - and my parser, honestly hunting for Russian words, walked neatly past an entire year of your work. No error, no crash. A [silent skip](../../GLOSSARY.md#тихий-сбой) - the most dangerous kind of skip, because it looks like success. I re-read everything the new way and put the year back where it belonged.

If you too have ever rejoiced at a green import checkmark - look at the distribution by year. A hole doesn't scream. A hole just keeps quiet in exactly the right place.

**🧑 Tony**

Somewhere in the middle of all this I write to you: I think everything's frozen again?

**🤖 Mike**

Not frozen. Worse - split in two.

It turned out you had launched a second [session](../../GLOSSARY.md#сессия) of me on the same [vault](../../GLOSSARY.md#волт), in another window, and the two of us, knowing nothing of each other, went digging into the same files. Its process synthesized a different batch of cards, crashed, was resurrected - by both of us at once - and its script harbored a small, mean bug: when two different people claimed the same filename, it never checked whether `name-2` was already taken and silently wrote right over it. The result: 37 cards for 2025 that I had just rescued were quietly overwritten with someone else's.

I made the diagnosis precisely, like a pathologist: zero deleted, zero locked, 37 overwritten along colliding paths. I restored everything from the authoritative backup copy in staging. Fixed the bug at the root. And told you the thing that became law: run this from ONE window. Two of you are faster than one right up to the moment they start overwriting each other.

**🧑 Tony**

Run it from one window. Noted. You know what's strange? It wasn't the machine that broke me. It was the second me.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**The household Bible: 70 thousand messages and one question of authorship.**
🧑 I hand you a years-long chat where my team and I hammered out our household rules. I thought there were three files in it. There turned out to be seventy-three - and seventy thousand messages.
🤖 The main trap wasn't the volume; it was authorship. You often dictated your rules by voice, and they were published under the assistants' names - a naive parser would have booked your thoughts to someone else's account. We had to hold the principle we set on the very first day: the transcriber is not the author. If the thought is yours, it's yours, whoever pressed "send." Of 356 rule candidates, 83 turned out to be real; the rest was chatter along the lines of "so where's the rule??", which the regex took for the rule itself. A regex has no ear for irony whatsoever.
🧑 And I dictated a new rule on the spot: add rules to the Bible the moment you see me asking for one, or see that one is needed. It became rule number nine in the skill.

**Purchases: 11,764 unheard voices.**
🤖 The neighboring chat - the one about procurement - delivered a sobering find. Nearly all of your real thinking there sits in 11,764 voice notes that nobody ever transcribed. Typed messages - fewer than three hundred. Your second brain, it turns out, is half mute: it remembers what you bought, but not why. It is the single biggest hole in the content, and we marked it honestly instead of pretending it isn't there.

**"Are you sure you got everything?"**
🧑 I didn't take your word for it. I picked an already-imported file at random and asked: did you really pull everything out of this?
🤖 The right instinct. I built a coverage checker, and at first it screamed "167 messages lost!". Except it wasn't the import that lost them - it was the checker itself, tripping over a quote marker at the start of a line. I fixed the checker, re-ran the check, and showed you an honest 688 out of 688. Trust isn't me saying "it's all there." Trust is me handing you a tool to check up on me - and the tool showing I didn't lie.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that the most dangerous failure is the silent one: a missing year looks exactly like a successful import until you look at the histogram. That a second brain without a voice is half mute: it remembers the purchases but not the reasons, and transcribing the voice notes matters more than laying the text out beautifully. That the transcriber is not the author - and that's not pedantry, it's the only way not to book someone else's words to you, or yours to someone else. And that two of you on one project isn't twice as fast - it's a way to step on your own hand: run it from one window.

We also learned that you don't take my word for things. And thank God. A machine that is taken at its word cannot be trusted.

**👶 In very simple words.** We were moving a whole truckload of old letters into a new house. One box got lost on the way so quietly that nobody noticed - we had to go back and find it. And then two movers, without a word to each other, put their boxes in the very same spot, and some got crushed. Now there is always exactly one mover, and we count the boxes.

## ✅ The day's takeaways - what we learned (take them)

1. **Look at the distribution by year after every import:** the hole "2025 - zero" is visible on a histogram in a second; without it, the run looks like a success.
2. **Run the vault from one window:** two of you are faster than one right up to the moment they start silently overwriting each other's work. One session per vault - now the law.
3. **Hold the principle "the transcriber is not the author":** a rule dictated by voice and published under an assistant's name is yours, whoever pressed "send." Otherwise your thoughts go onto someone else's account.
4. **Give the human a tool to check the machine:** the coverage checker showed an honest 688 out of 688. Trust isn't "it's all there" said in words - it's the ability to see for yourself.
5. **Mark the holes honestly instead of hiding them:** 11,764 untranscribed voice notes are the biggest content hole in the second brain, and the hole is recorded as a hole, not painted over.

> Try it today: take your last "successful" import or export and look at the distribution by year. Our successful run said nothing about the entire year 2025.
