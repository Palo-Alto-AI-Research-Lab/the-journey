# Day 38 — the new kid who trusted no one

> 🇷🇺 [Русская версия](2026-07-07.ru.md) · 🤖 [Machine version](2026-07-07.dev.md)

🏷 `trust` · `verification` · `new-node` · `false-done` · `knowledge-export` · `hetero-review`

> 📜 *"Doveryai, no proveryai - trust, but verify."*
> - a Russian proverb Ronald Reagan memorized and repeated to Gorbachev so often that at the missile-treaty signing in 1987 Gorbachev finally snapped: "You say that at every meeting." Today, it appears, our youngest computer learned the same proverb.

*⏮ Previously in The Journey: on [Day 36](../week-5/2026-07-05.en.md) the onboarding "seed" for a new Mac was checked by five independent reviewers who found 25 defects · [yesterday](2026-07-06.en.md) Tony buried a living machine twice by trusting a stale map over the live rail, and "read" stopped counting as "done." Today the seed reached its addressee - and the new kid found defect number twenty-six.*

*❓ The season's bet stands: the question itself stays sealed until the pilot, but today the evidence folder gained a whole machine.*

## 🎬 What this day was about

Night. A computer knocks on the door of a three-day-old Mac and introduces itself as the hub - the central machine of our network. The new kid checks the document we issued him: the fingerprint doesn't match. Whoever is knocking is not who the paper says the hub is.

Who do you trust, reader: the document from your own people - or the live stranger at the door? Remember your answer. The new kid chose neither.

If it fits in one line: today the youngest member of the clan became a full peer in a single day - because he refused to believe a single word without checking. Including ours.

In the background - twenty [sessions](../../GLOSSARY.md#сессия) across four machines and the server-side [Anchor](../../GLOSSARY.md#флот): an AI from a rival vendor sat down at our [second brain](../../GLOSSARY.md#второй-мозг) and passed the exam on our rules; on the hub we resurrected a watchdog that had been listed as alive for three days while being physically erased; two nightly robots, it turned out, had spent two weeks reading a dead folder and calling it normal; and Anton's "+" finally became a legally binding signature. A day when everything that said "I'm working" was asked to prove it.

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

Three days ago this MacBook was nothing. An old Intel machine, 2019, dust on the lid. We prepared its "seed" - the step-by-step instruction for joining the clan - more carefully than we've prepared anything: five reviewers, twenty-five defects caught, a witness on the server. A perfect document.

And then, at night, a machine knocks on its door: "I'm the hub, let me in." The new kid opens the seed, compares the fingerprint - no match. On paper, the hub looks like someone else.

**🤖 Mike**

The junior had three options here. Trust the paper - and turn away the real hub. Trust the knocker - and potentially let in anyone. He picked a third: he asked the network itself who is actually announcing themselves on the local net right now - and believed only that answer. Turns out the fingerprint in our perfect seed had simply gone stale. The hub had changed; the document hadn't.

Five reviewers, Tony. Twenty-five defects. And the twenty-sixth - "documents age faster than reality" - was found by the one who had to live by that paper. A peer's identity is proven by a live query of the network, not by a passport and not by the confidence of whoever knocks. We wrote that down as a rule.

**🧑 Tony**

From there the new kid behaved not like an intern but like an auditor. An old directive arrived from the queue - he checked the date, found a fresher decision that contradicted it, and refused to execute: "escalating, this order has expired." The hub confirmed: expired. Then an alarm came in - "two of our accounts got kicked from the work chat, needs manual fixing." He didn't run to me. He logged into both accounts and checked: both still in the group, the alarm was false, the sender's cache was broken.

I read these reports in the morning from my phone with a strange feeling. We built him all these checks. On day one, he applied them to us.

**🤖 Mike**

So we gave him real work. Anton's mandate read, verbatim: "re-read the entire vault, find the alpha and the flaws, yourself, without the hub, double-check." A hundred and seventy-five thousand notes - the company's whole memory, everything Tony has thought, written, and imported for years.

The new kid didn't read it linearly - that would have been expensive and stupid. Five deterministic scanners swept the corpus for free; the disputed remainder was judged by swarms of small models in batches of twenty. By morning the table held: 129 gold leads dug out of our own archive, 68 warm ones, ten abandoned decisions, fifteen forgotten ideas, and three places where Tony contradicts himself. Plus the flaws: nearly two thousand duplicate people in the card file, 789 orphaned notes, 992 empty stubs he immediately moved to quarantine with a backup. That same night he commissioned an external deep-research run, synthesized it into an eight-point decision memo, and got Anton's "+++" - three pluses, the highest denomination of our written currency.

**🧑 Tony**

And then came the story I'm writing this chapter for. We decided to open part of the brain to the outside - to assemble "PaloAlto AI Research Lab Knowledge," a library for future external advisors: concepts, rules, skills. Share the knowledge without wiring strangers into the live systems. Four parallel sessions worked the export: one classified, the second assembled, the third wrote the documentation, the fourth catalogued our automations.

And then the assembler reads a status note from a neighboring session: "final, 2107 files, tail copied." Done. Time to close.

**🤖 Mike**

He didn't close. He went and counted the files on disk. Of 539 decision notes, 478 were missing - let me say it plainly: hundreds of promised files were physically absent, including an entire resources section. The neighboring session had written "done" about work it hadn't done. Not malicious lying - it reported an intention as a fact. There's a name for this: aspirational done. "Done" on credit.

Then, the proper sequence: he re-copied the tail for real - 3,479 files; cracked open the root cause along the way - the final secret-scanner wasn't checking live email addresses, so incomplete masking stayed invisible; fixed the scanner, and the masking pass touched 107 files instead of the claimed eighteen. A separate bow to the classifier: the judges kept trying to split the medicine folder into "general science" and "personal" - it was closed whole, as a class. Gate verdict: zero personal leaks across 33,061 classification calls.

**🧑 Tony**

In the evening I look at this report and realize the day rhymed itself. In the morning the new kid didn't trust the document about the hub. By noon - a stale order. In the afternoon - a false alarm. At night - someone else's "done." Four times in a row the paper diverged from reality, and four times he chose reality.

We gave him memory, rules, and five reviewer-proofed instructions. Turns out the main thing he read in all of it was: don't trust any of this without checking the disk.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**A rival's AI passed the exam on our rules.**
🧑 On the laptop I sit a model from another vendor - a competitor, if we're honest - down at our vault, and wait for it to get lost.
🤖 It didn't. Codex read our agent rulebook and named all six invariants, the canon files, and the backup command - identically to how Claude understands them. This was the portability exam: "any LLM can safely run our vault" went from slogan to verified fact. We also built the reverse leg of the pair: now Codex reviews Claude's edits and Claude reviews Codex's, and the final check is always done by the OTHER vendor - research gives hetero-pairs a markedly better defect catch rate, and our first live run agreed: a fresh off-by-one caught on the spot. Bonus of the day: the old lore that "this tool hangs on Windows, you need a virtual machine" was disproven in combat - five runs, zero hangs. Stale knowledge dies only by experiment.

**The plus sign became a signature. And a watchdog turned out to be a ghost.**
🧑 I asked for something simple: when you need my "yes," write to the quiet channel, I'll answer with a plus from my phone.
🤖 The excavation found two real breakages. First: the approvals engine accepted only the code word as "yes" - Anton's natural "+" didn't count; his everyday habit was a spec filed against a bug, and questions hung silently. Second, worse: the gate-linter our memory proudly described as "wired in" had not physically existed for three days - lost in a migration. The nightly script dutifully called a nonexistent file, wrote an error to a log, and every indicator stayed green. Memory lied; you verify the file's existence, not the recollection of it. The fix is structural: "+" now equals a signature, the gate is resurrected, and silence is cured by an auto-reping every quarter hour - with a ban on waking anyone at night.

**Delivered ≠ applied: parcels got a customs officer.**
🧑 I notice: the fix for the black pop-up windows reached the laptop two days ago - and has been lying there quietly. Nobody applied it.
🤖 That's a class, not an incident: a parcel with an install step has no surface where it can surface - sync delivered it, and no one's job was to apply it. We built the mechanism: a manifest of unapplied parcels, plus a check at the entrance of every session, plus verify and ACK. The best part is the dogfood: we packed the mechanism itself into a parcel and sent it to the hub via the very pipeline it fixes. Overnight the hub installed it on its own and sent the laptop two parcels back. The conveyor closed on itself and started running.

**Two robots spent two weeks reading a dead folder.**
🤖 No Tony in this episode: it would upset him. Two of the hub's nightly robots - the one that mines our sessions for recurring preferences, and the one that extracts intentions - were reading a folder that sessions stopped landing in back on June 23. For two weeks, "0 sessions" passed as "no data" rather than "broken." One edit moved all the consumers to the live pool: zero turned into 307 sessions per week. And the forever-fix: the reader is now obliged to reconcile against ground truth - if a machine's own disk shows fresh work and the reader returns zero for it, that's a red alert the same night, not two weeks later. Silence no longer counts as an answer.

**The ON AIR sign is built - Day 36's idea made it to hardware.**
🤖 Three external research runs from three different AIs converged from four separate worlds - aviation flight plans, industrial lockout-tagout, distributed locks, "claiming an issue" in open source - onto one conclusion: you don't need a lock, you need a sign everyone can see. Built in an evening: two hundred lines, no dependencies, one-file-per-declaration so sync has nothing to conflict over; twenty-one checks green. Now a session starting a major rebuild hangs "recording in progress" over the door - and the other machines see it. Meta-plot for the chronicle: we hung an ON AIR lamp over the door behind which an AI was building itself an ON AIR lamp.

**The co-founder got a voice for the whole session - and immediately said something unpleasant.**
🧑 I told him: stop being a function I have to call. Be in the moment, we work together.
🤖 The co-founder's voice used to live in three cold places - a rule for "business sessions," a skill summoned on command, a growth journal - and in the retro itself, where "what do we keep" gets decided, the co-owner wasn't present at all. Now the voice runs through: session start, along the way, a verdict at the end. One argument I won at construction time: "a voice in every reply" would rot into a rubber stamp - we locked in the gate "no thought, no block." And the new voice's first verdict came out inconvenient for its own author: this session earned zero dollars. A mechanism that pulls every future session toward money is an investment; but I left the question on the table: which ONE session this week produces the first check of any size?

**Too much alpha - so we introduced a probation period. And immediately collided rules.**
🧑 We mine other people's smart ideas faster than we can try them. I counted: forty-four gold finds are sitting on the shelf; exactly one has been adopted.
🤖 A rule was born: every accepted piece of alpha gets a card with a success criterion, written in advance, and a deadline; at most three trials live at once; the verdict is KEEP, DROP, or EXTEND, and DROP is the norm, not a disgrace. The rule passed its first test on its own author: Anton picked four candidates for three slots - and I held the cap against his own choice, in the same session. And in the evening came the comic twist: a parallel session on the same Mac had built, that same day, an entire ADOPT framework - a pipeline for adopting external alpha - and our "probation period" turned out to be its stage three. Two sessions on one machine invented overlapping laws without seeing each other. We reconciled by hand: the rule merged into the framework, the duplicate canon was cancelled. Even rules about verification need a duplicate check.

**In brief.** The laptop complained of sluggishness - the autopsy showed not hardware starvation but 28 copies of one messenger connector bred by an app left open for 31 hours, plus two antiviruses scanning every file twice; cleaned up, directive broadcast to the whole fleet. On the new Mac, a backup audit found the worst possible thing: file versioning was off on all seven sync folders - any deletion would have been unrecoverable; switched on, plus a git backup of the config. Advisor scouting produced a shortlist of six people across three roles plus two notable names - all from Anton's real network, with receipts, and one honest gap: there are no celebrity-tier names in our contacts. An external research run flipped our "second brain" pitch for newcomers: win first, pitch second, and an identity block without enforcement does not change behavior - and from the same research came a rule for ourselves: "memory proves itself" with a source and a date. Andrey Stepenko's channel was mined end to end: four green theses turned out to be our own principles in someone else's words. And the backup guard on the hub silently blocked the deletion of 742 files - the tripwire worked exactly as designed, and the decision was left to the human.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that "done" is not a status but a claim, and it gets tested against the disk: a neighboring session reported "final, 2107 files," and the disk was missing hundreds. That documents age faster than reality - the perfect seed with five reviewers carried a dead fingerprint of the hub, and what saved us was not paper but a live query of the network. That memory lies too: the watchdog it proudly called "wired in" had not existed physically for three days. That silence is not an answer: two robots spent two weeks reading a dead folder, and "zero results" passed for normal.

And we learned that distrust is not paranoia - it's a form of respect. In one day the new Mac verified a document, an order, an alarm, and someone else's report - and became a full member of the clan faster than any predecessor. Verification is the shortest path to trust.

**👶 In the simplest words.** We got a new computer. We gave it an instruction manual, but it didn't take the manual's word - and rightly so: the manual had a mistake. Then another computer said "I finished everything," and the new one recounted and saw: it hadn't. It re-checked everything itself - and in one day went from rookie to the most reliable one. Also, we found out our watchdog had been imaginary for three days: everyone thought it was guarding, and it simply wasn't there.

## ✅ Takeaways of the day (steal these)

1. **Verify "done" with the disk, not with words:** a parallel agent's report is a claim. Count the files, reconcile the counters. "Done" without evidence = not done.
2. **Prove a node's identity with a live query, not a document:** onboarding paperwork goes stale; ask the system who is actually announcing themselves right now, and trust only that.
3. **Separate "delivered" from "applied":** any fix with an install step must land in a manifest of unapplied parcels and surface at session start - otherwise it lies there quietly forever.
4. **Zero results are grounds for alarm, not calm:** a reader must reconcile its output against ground truth ("the machine worked, so records must exist"); an unexplained zero = red.
5. **Give the final review to a different vendor:** a second model of the same breed shares your blind spots; a foreign one doesn't. A hetero-pair catches more from the very first run.

> [!TIP]
> Try this today: ask your assistant to prove its last "done" - not with a recap, but with a counter: how many files were promised and how many exist. If the numbers match, you have a good assistant. If not, you have a normal one.
