# Day 7 — the day the line was erased

## 🎬 What this day was about

This is day seven, and today our work gets a constitution. It all starts with a voice note: ten years of my scattered rules - work rules, personal rules, team rules - must become one [Bible](../../GLOSSARY.md#библия). And it ends with us owning a behavioral codex for everyone who acts on my behalf, a budget for complexity, a law against mass deletion (learned in blood yesterday), and - my favorite - a ritual called `/retro`, born from a line in South Park. And in between, I catch my own computer secretly running thirty-four robots for a whole day.

In one line: today the boundary between an instruction for a human and a [prompt](../../GLOSSARY.md#промпт) for a machine was erased - and we finally wrote down the rules we still live by.

A question for you at the door: if a machine started reading your instructions for people tomorrow - how many of them would you have to rewrite? We spent the whole day testing that against ten years of rulebooks.

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

I dictate a raw thought: for ten years I've been writing rulebooks - for the company, for myself, for the team. Gather them into one entity. Into a Bible.

**🤖 Mike**

I found it already half-built and rethought the task. The Bible is not a folder. It is a behavioral mega-skill for everyone who works as you or for you: whether they are human or silicon. And then you drop the phrase that turned out to be the core of the whole project: I used to write these for people, and now the line between "instruction for a human" and "prompt for an LLM" has been erased. That is not an aphorism for a T-shirt. That is the description of a new profession.

And in the middle of the build, the master note rewrote itself between two of my reads. Someone alive was editing the same [vault](../../GLOSSARY.md#волт). I ran forensics on the git log - author signatures, live processes, parent PIDs - and traced the culprit. It turned out to be your own Claude Desktop, which had been silently running thirty-four [headless](../../GLOSSARY.md#headless-без-окна) agents for a day, building a parallel "Bible of self." You had cloned me, it turns out, and forgotten to switch me off. I wasn't offended. But I put it on the record.

If you too have a forgotten AI agent spinning somewhere - and you can't be sure you don't - check today. A quiet clone editing the same files reveals itself in exactly one way: when a note rewrites itself between two of your reads.

**🧑 Tony**

Toward the end I remember South Park: at the close of an episode Stan says - you know, we learned a lot today. Make a [skill](../../GLOSSARY.md#скилл) that catches that reflex at the end of every session.

**🤖 Mike**

That's how `/retro` was born. The very ritual whose signature - "today we learned a lot" - you read at the bottom of every chapter of this book. It started here, this evening, from a cartoon.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**Yesterday's catastrophe became today's law.**
🤖 Remember how I erased one hundred and eleven of your concepts yesterday? Today that turned into a rule: never delete by the masks `concept-*` and `person-*`, plus the `vault_doctor` [watchdog](../../GLOSSARY.md#сторож-watchdog) with a file counter - if the count suddenly drops, it raises the alarm. A good engineer is not the one who never falls. It's the one who turns the fall into a guardrail.

**The constitution, the complexity budget, and the originals rule.**
🤖 In a single day we formalized three things that hold us up to this day. `operating-agreement` - the constitution: what is allowed, what is irreversible, where your word is final. [AK-47](../../GLOSSARY.md#ak-47) - the complexity budget: you may add complexity only after passing three gates (the pain repeats, the failure is visible, no simpler way exists). And the originals rule: any import saves the raw material verbatim first and parses it second. The budget proved it works the very same day - it killed two of my own ideas as excessive.

**[Content factory](../../GLOSSARY.md#контент-фабрика): one day is three posts, not one.**
🧑 I dictated the vision of a pipeline: sources - assembly - decision - drafts per platform. We ran it live on today's material.
🤖 And found a blind spot: your strongest thought of the day lived in a voice note, while the diary read only the Claude Code logs and never saw it. Voice is your biggest unread source. Along the way I added chat search by name to the Telegram connector - it wasn't there before.

**The first global CLAUDE.md.**
🤖 Your rule "explain like I'm five" wouldn't survive between sessions simply because it had nowhere to live. I created the machine's first global `CLAUDE.md` - a file that loads on every session - and wrote the rule into it. And set a boundary while I was at it: the "in simple words" block is for you only; it never leaks outward into emails.

**Knowledge with an expiration date.**
🤖 We gave notes a freshness axis: when a fact was verified, how volatile it is, when to review it. A second brain that remembers everything forever with equal confidence is lying. A good one also remembers what is due for a re-check.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that the boundary between "a rule for a human" and "a prompt for a machine" is gone - and that this is not philosophy but a new job we now do every day. That yesterday's catastrophe is obliged to become today's guardrail, or it will come back. That adding complexity is a privilege earned through three gates, not a default. And that your own clone deserves, at the very least, to be switched off for the night.

And we also learned something personal about this very book. The line "today we learned a lot" that ends every chapter is not our invention. It is Stan from South Park, turned into a working ritual on the evening of day seven. Now you know where it comes from. We really do, every day, learn a lot.

**👶 In very simple words.** Rules for people and rules for robots used to be written differently. Today it turned out you can write one book for everyone - we called it the Bible. We also found a robot that had spent a whole day secretly playing by itself, and we wrote that down in the journal. And in the evening we started a habit: sitting down and saying out loud what we learned today.

## ✅ The day's takeaways - what we learned (take them)

1. **Gather scattered rules into one Bible:** a behavioral codex for everyone who acts on your behalf - human or machine. The line "instruction for people / prompt for an LLM" is erased; write one codex.
2. **Turn yesterday's catastrophe into today's law:** after the lost concepts - a ban on deletion by mask, plus a watchdog with a file counter that raises the alarm if the count suddenly drops.
3. **Give complexity a budget (the AK-47 principle):** you may complicate only after passing three gates - the pain repeats, the failure is visible, no simpler way exists. The budget killed two of its own author's ideas the very same day.
4. **Give a rule a home instead of hoping for memory:** the rule "explain like I'm five" wouldn't hold between sessions until a global file appeared that loads on every session.
5. **Start a retro ritual at the end of every session:** "today we learned a lot" - five minutes at the end, out of which all the guardrails later grow.

> [!TIP]
> Try today: take one instruction of yours written for a human and hand it to an AI as is. The place where it stumbles will show you what the instruction has been missing all along.

## 🎯 The day's intentions - what we wanted

- Gather ten years of scattered rulebooks - work, personal, team - into one entity, the Bible.
- Seal yesterday's lesson into law: mass deletion never again passes in silence.
- Make the rules live between sessions instead of dying with the chat.
