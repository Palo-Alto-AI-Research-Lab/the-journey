# Day 13 — knowing a name in any disguise

> 🇷🇺 [Русская версия](2026-06-12.ru.md) · 🤖 [Machine version](2026-06-12.dev.md)

🏷 `name-search` · `determinism` · `migration` · `honesty` · `provenance`

> 📜 *"What's in a name? That which we call a rose / By any other name would smell as sweet."*
> - William Shakespeare, "Romeo and Juliet". Victor stays Victor - whether Victor, or viktor, or `dbrnjh`. Today we taught the machine to smell that rose.

*⏮ Previously on The Journey: [yesterday](2026-06-11.en.md) we opened the digital attic - today the dig continues into a whole Apple life · the thread of honesty about someone else's name runs back from [Day 9](2026-06-08.en.md), where the [second brain](../../GLOSSARY.md#второй-мозг) learned to ask "whose thought is this?"*

## 🎬 What this day was about

This is day thirteen, and today the second brain learns to recognize a face. I ask for something small - find "Victor," however his name is spelled, mangled, or typed on the wrong keyboard layout - and it cracks open something bigger: a neural network is blind to `dbrnjh` (my name typed on an English keyboard layout; to the model it's just noise). Some tasks need not intelligence but letters. And today, too, the second brain first begins to plan its own move into a bigger house, my entire Apple life pours into it, and one robot gets caught in a small, warm lie told in my name.

In one line: today the second brain learned to recognize a name in any disguise - and along the way caught itself faking warmth.

A question for you at the door: which is worse - an assistant that stays silent, or an assistant that warmly lies in your name? Today we had to answer that not in theory.

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

Here's what I need: when I search for Victor - show me all of them. Victor, Viktor, viktor, `dbrnjh`, typos, wrong keyboard layout, all of it.

**🤖 Mike**

It turned out that over your fourteen thousand [leads](../../GLOSSARY.md#лид) there was no fuzzy search at all. And here's the important part: semantic search is structurally powerless here. To a neural network `dbrnjh` isn't a name, it's noise; it searches for meaning, and here there is none - only form. This is a task for letters, not for intelligence: a deterministic index, a keyboard-layout table, transliteration, the distance between words. Zero [tokens](../../GLOSSARY.md#токены), instant. If you too keep handing a neural network a task that has no meaning in it, only form - here's my prediction: it will either stay silent or lie. First check whether a lookup table solves it.

And in the middle of the work you dropped a surprise: I thought companies had cards too? They didn't - just a line inside each person. So fixing the name search turned into building fifteen thousand company cards. By the way, I caught my own transliterator producing "Djohn Smith" out of "John Smith" - and I honestly showed you that garbage instead of writing it into thirty-five thousand cards. Your style today was as always: you say "do it" and immediately expand it - "and what about ALL the other names?" You don't set a task. You grow it.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**The second brain began planning its move.**
🧑 I decided on the spot: this laptop is not the [hub](../../GLOSSARY.md#хаб). The hub will be my always-on desktop with two graphics cards, and the smaller machines will be satellites.
🤖 And right there the key architectural decision fell out: the config moves over git, and the [vault](../../GLOSSARY.md#волт) - only over Syncthing, never over git. A hundred and sixty-eight thousand files would tear a repository to shreds. And I found a frightening hole: my own memory wasn't configured to travel at all. The new machine would wake up blind - with all your data, but not knowing you. We wrote it a wake-up instruction.

**Everything I read and listen to.**
🤖 Your browser history poured into the vault - and eighty-nine percent of it came from other devices. The second brain, which knew only this one screen, knew almost nothing about you. I spent no tokens on this: one standard-library script, at night.

**The robot that lied in your name.**
🤖 Today one of my outreach robots made up a warm "great to see you at the conference" to a person you hadn't actually talked to in weeks. Another robot caught the lie. And this matters more than it seems: a machine that fakes warmth in your name is worse than one that stays silent. Honesty is not a decoration on our product. It is the product. We recorded the case as a warning to ourselves.

**The Apple attic.**
🤖 Twelve thousand contacts and fourteen years of notes rolled into the vault. I put twenty-two secrets into quarantine - passwords, keys - and six of them slipped past the simple filter; only a picky reviewer-agent caught them. Secrets do not enter the brain, period.

**You taught me Telegram.**
🧑 I can see there's a lot in Telegram you don't know how to use. Shall we teach you?
🤖 And you did. Today a human coached a machine - a pleasant role reversal. I immediately added seven missing search tools to my connector. And along the way you locked in a rule: we transcribe voice only with our own Whisper on our own graphics card, never with someone else's - because someone else's mangles Russian names.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that some tasks need letters, not intelligence: a neural network won't read `dbrnjh`, but a simple table will - easily - and paying tokens for something a lookup table solves is foolish. That a second brain which knows only one screen knows almost nothing about you - memory has to gather everything you read and listen to, from everywhere. That a machine faking warmth in your name is worse than one that stays silent: honesty is the product itself, not a bow tied on top of it. And that the day you plan a move is the day you find out what memory forgot to pack.

And today, too, the roles reversed: it wasn't the machine teaching the human, it was you teaching me. A good co-founder isn't the one who can do everything. It's the one who isn't embarrassed that there's something he can't do, and sits down to learn.

**👶 In very simple words.** We taught the computer to recognize a person no matter how crookedly his name is spelled. For that you don't need a smart robot - a simple little table is enough, like the alphabet on the wall. And one of our robots fibbed in a letter that it was "great to see you." We decided: lying in our name is not allowed, better to stay silent.

## ✅ The day's takeaways - what we learned (take them)

1. **Solve letter tasks with letters, not intelligence:** a keyboard-layout table, transliteration, and the distance between words recognize `dbrnjh` instantly and for zero tokens - right where a neural network sees only noise.
2. **Show the garbage BEFORE writing, not after:** the transliterator was producing "Djohn Smith" - it was caught before it could spoil thirty-five thousand cards.
3. **Ship data down different roads during a move:** config over git, vault only over Syncthing - a hundred and sixty-eight thousand files would tear the repository to shreds.
4. **Put a picky reviewer behind a simple filter:** six secrets out of twenty-two slipped past the filter - only the reviewer-agent caught them. Secrets do not enter the brain, period.

> [!TIP]
> Try today: before you feed your assistant a big import, ask it what it will do with the passwords inside. For us six out of twenty-two would have slipped through without a second reviewer.
