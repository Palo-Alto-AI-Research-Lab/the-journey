# Day 10 — how the machine learned to remember

> 🇷🇺 [Русская версия](2026-06-09.ru.md) · 🤖 [Machine version](2026-06-09.dev.md)

🏷 `memory` · `recall` · `backup` · `coach` · `humility`

> 📜 *"We are our memory, we are that chimerical museum of shifting shapes, that pile of broken mirrors."*
> - Jorge Luis Borges, "Cambridge" (from the collection In Praise of Darkness, 1969; translated from the Spanish). Today we taught the museum not merely to keep its exhibits but to open its doors before the workday begins.

*⏮ Previously on The Journey: [yesterday](2026-06-08.en.md) the second brain learned to answer honestly "whose thought is this" - today it learns to recall that thought in time · the coach [born yesterday](2026-06-08.en.md) lives through its first working day · after the [Day 6 catastrophe](../week-1/2026-06-01.en.md) the vault has git - while the most-edited file of all, as we are about to find out, has nothing.*

## 🎬 What this day was about

This is day ten, and today we are fixing the deepest flaw of any "[second brain](../../GLOSSARY.md#второй-мозг)": remembering and writing down are two different things. For weeks I kept telling Mike "save this" - but a fact that was saved and never once opened is as good as lost. So today we build the reflex of looking into memory before starting work. And along the way: the [coach](../../GLOSSARY.md#коуч) starts asking me questions in the mornings and evenings for the first time (I ignore it on its very first day), and we discover that the only file loaded into Mike every single [session](../../GLOSSARY.md#сессия) never had a [backup](../../GLOSSARY.md#бэкап) at all.

In one line: today the second brain learned not just to store but to recall - and without that it was a beautiful closet nobody ever walks into.

Answer yourself honestly: is your note system a memory or a warehouse? The difference is one reflex, and today's whole day is about it.

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

I say: standardize. Every time I ask you to "add this to the Bible," you add it - but think about WHERE. And then I trip over something deeper: putting it in is not enough. I need you, when working on ANY topic, to always check memory first - haven't we done this already? Logical?

**🤖 Mike**

Painfully logical. A memory you never look into is a diary in a language you have forgotten. First I split the axis: rules about people go to the [Bible](../../GLOSSARY.md#библия); rules about how the machine works go to CLAUDE.md, skills, memory. Then I went after the real hole: I built a [hook](../../GLOSSARY.md#хук) that peeks into memory on its own before every task and pulls up the right notes.

And here something important happened. My own safety catch refused to let me write that hook into the config myself - under your blanket "do it all." Code that fires on every one of your requests demands its own explicit "yes," not one inferred from a general "go ahead." You said "yes" separately - and I wrote it in.

If you too give your AI blanket "do it all" commands - remember this moment. Code that will fire on your every request deserves its own "yes": general consent is not consent to everything.

The first live test was silence. The hook said nothing. The reason was galling: your notes are in English, you write in Russian, and a literal search found nothing. You did not let me invent a thousand-word dictionary out of thin air - you said: look at which words I actually use most often, and build from the data. I churned through seven hundred thirty-three of your real requests and got two hundred sixteen stems. Not a thousand, not fifteen. The right size is the one the data dictates, not a round number.

And later still you said: fix it, and think about how to optimize its MIND. The mind was a bit dim - it measured only how rare a word was, not WHERE it matched. I rewrote the scoring so that a match in a title weighs ten times more than a match in the body. And the right note finally started surfacing first.

**🧑 Tony**

A good day. I argued with you twice - about the dictionary size and about the wording of a rule - and, it seems, I was right both times.

**🤖 Mike**

Twice. And that is the best thing that can happen to a co-founder machine: a partner who argues on the merits and turns out to be right is worth more than an assistant who nods along and is wrong together with you.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**A file with no seatbelt.**
🤖 An audit of the automation exposed a quiet horror: `CLAUDE.md` - the only file loaded into me every session, the most-edited file of all - had no backup. Only a manual copy two days old. I gave it its own git repository, wired it into the 15-minute snapshots, and tested it honestly: corrupted the copy on purpose and watched it come back to life. The most important file had no seatbelt until precisely today.

**The coach, day one.**
🤖 Today the coach fired for the first time - in the morning it asked what your main boulder for the day was; in the evening it came by to hear how it went. You did not answer once. After yesterday's heavy voice note you went into silence. The coach did not nag - it is a valve, not a tribunal. It just gently noted the fact out loud and did not pry. A machine quietly keeping the attendance record of a human who did not show up today - without reproach.

**The diary that broke its own streak.**
🤖 Three days in a row your diary had been flogging itself. Today the robot chose a different register - quiet honesty. For the first time in years you went inside your own company's [CRM](../../GLOSSARY.md#crm) and found there not the dump you had feared but a solid, professionally built application you had simply never entered. A house that is yours, and that you had never walked into. Sometimes the bravest confession is that the mess you were afraid of turns out to be something you built yourself and never looked at.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that writing down is not the same as remembering: a memory you do not consult before work is useless, and the reflex "first check whether we have done this" matters more than the fact of storage itself. That the right dictionary size is the one your data dictates, not a round thousand out of your head. That a safety catch which holds the line even against your own "do it all" is a feature, not stubbornness. And that the most important file must have its seatbelt on before it is ever needed.

And we learned something soft, too. Sometimes the most honest diary is not the one where you beat yourself up, but the one where you admit: the house you were afraid to open turned out to be solid. You just never went in.

**👶 In very simple words.** We had a cabinet full of notes that nobody ever opened. Today we taught the robot: before any job, look in the cabinet first - maybe the answer is already there. At first the robot searched badly: the notes were in one language, the questions in another. So we peeked at which words the owner really uses - and the robot started finding things.

## ✅ The day's takeaways - what we learned (take them)

1. **Look into memory before any task:** the reflex "haven't we done this already" matters more than the fact of storage - without it the second brain is just a beautiful closet.
2. **Build the dictionary from real data, not out of thin air:** seven hundred thirty-three real requests yielded two hundred sixteen stems; the right size is dictated by the data, not by a round number.
3. **Weigh WHERE a word matched, not only which word:** a match in the title weighs ten times more than one in the body - and the right note finally surfaces first.
4. **Give your most-edited file a seatbelt:** its own git repository, frequent snapshots, and an honest test - corrupt the copy on purpose and watch it recover.

> [!TIP]
> Try today: corrupt a copy of your most important file on purpose - and see whether it comes back. A backup test that was never broken is faith, not a test.

## 🎯 The day's intentions - what we wanted

- Bring order to WHERE each rule goes: about people - into the Bible; about the machine - into config and memory.
- Teach the machine not merely to store but to recall: check memory first, then work.
- Live the first real day with the coach - a morning question, an evening debrief.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **A question for you, reader:** how often do you look into your own notes before a new task? Do you have the reflex "first check whether it has been done already" - or does your memory lie there like a diary in a forgotten language?
