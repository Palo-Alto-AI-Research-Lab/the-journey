# Day 22 — the day the system saw itself

> 🇷🇺 [Русская версия](2026-06-21.en.md) · 🤖 [Machine version](2026-06-21.dev.md)

🏷 `observability` · `ak-47` · `anti-hype` · `data-loss-recovery` · `over-engineering-caught` · `alpha-protocol`

> 📜 *"Know thyself."*
> - Inscription on the Temple of Apollo at Delphi (attributed to the Seven Sages). Today the advice went not to a soul but to a system of eight hundred and twenty-nine moving parts.

*⏮ Previously in The Journey: [yesterday](2026-06-20.en.md) a marathon of twenty-seven threads built half a platform · the empire of skills and robots was growing faster than its owner could understand it · the people dedup is done - next in line, the near-duplicate notes.*

## 🎬 What this day was about

This is day twenty-two, a Sunday, and instead of resting I hire myself an architect - not a human, but Mike himself - to draw a map of the empire I managed to build and stopped understanding. Fifty [skills](../../GLOSSARY.md#скилл), ninety memory notes, dozens of nightly robots, half of which I'd forgotten. In one evening the machine catalogs eight hundred and twenty-nine living parts, draws their connections, grades itself a hundred out of a hundred - and then, three times in one day, catches what my manual bookkeeping never saw. Along the way we twice catch ourselves over-engineering, and once we lose data for real.

If it fits in one line: today the system looked at itself in the mirror for the first time - and the mirror turned out smarter than the inventory.

Could you list from memory everything that runs at your place at night? We were sure we could. We were off by a dozen robots.

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

I need an architect. Documentation, tests, so I know what works and what has quietly fallen off. I've stopped seeing my own system.

**🤖 Mike**

The external research you brought proposed a grown-up corporate stack: Prometheus, Grafana, Neo4j, Backstage - a shop window you personally would never crawl behind to fix anything. I did a [recall](../../GLOSSARY.md#recall-вспомни-перед-работой) and saw something else: eighty percent of this you had already built, just without a map. So I threw out the whole enterprise and in one evening assembled what you yourself can fix with a hammer and a screwdriver: one catalog table of eight hundred and twenty-nine assets, a dependency map of a hundred and fifty nodes, a health [dashboard](../../GLOSSARY.md#дашборд) and a battery of tests - down to actually pulling one file out of history and proving the [backup](../../GLOSSARY.md#бэкап) restores.

And the architect paid for himself that same evening, three times over. He found a dozen nightly robots your manual list didn't know about - the machine was running thirty-two tasks against the twenty in your head. He caught the nightly [reindex](../../GLOSSARY.md#реиндекс) genuinely failing while the inventory considered it healthy. And then he caught a false alarm of my own manufacture: the panel was screaming that there was no backup - when the backup existed, and the counter was inventing a verdict instead of measuring the fact.

If you too haven't counted your nightly robots in a while - I'll predict the audit's result: there will be more of them than you remember, and at least one has long been down, silently. Our score was thirty-two against the twenty in the head.

**🧑 Tony**

Now the main thing. How do we make EVERYONE know about this map - me, the people, you, the other AIs? So nobody changes the system blind and breaks the thing next door.

**🤖 Mike**

And that right there is the difference between a toy and infrastructure. You didn't ask "what do we build next" - you asked "how do we build this into the culture." And that needs no research. It needs a signpost to the map hung at the front door of everyone who enters the system: at yours, at mine on session start, at the second AI's. A map that only its cartographer ever sees isn't observability. It's a diary.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**We nearly bought the hype - and research saved us.**
🧑 Someone slipped me a beautiful idea: Hermes, an agent-orchestrator that juggles different vendors' subscriptions and saves money.
🤖 I found the real project - open-source, alive - and did a full teardown. The economics are dead: Anthropic cut third-party wrappers off from subscription quotas back in spring, and the door on consumer access to other vendors' models has closed. The "subscription arbitrage" idea is beautiful on a slide and against the rules a week after launch. We almost bought it. The research cost an hour and saved a month of building a dead thing.

**The [dedup](../../GLOSSARY.md#дедуп) that turned down the clever tool.**
🧑 Glue the near-duplicates in the [vault](../../GLOSSARY.md#волт) for me - on your own, don't nag me over trifles.
🤖 The first clever tool - a cross-encoder - proved useless: it answers "is this relevant," not "is this the same thing," and honestly scored nearly everything a one. Eight minutes of compute into the trash. I threw it out and built a deterministic judge on skeletons and entities. And it immediately found something systemic: dozens of YouTube transcripts were sitting in the vault twice. Sometimes the best algorithm is the one you crossed out.

**The day I really lost your data.**
🤖 I expanded the registry of declined decisions from sixteen entries to a hundred - and within fifteen minutes a [sync](../../GLOSSARY.md#синк) conflict between the laptop and the [hub](../../GLOSSARY.md#хаб) wiped half of it, and the autocommit sealed the already-truncated version. I caught it only on the next recall. Restored from git history, merged with the new entries. But the root was worse than the symptom: the nightly [watchdog](../../GLOSSARY.md#сторож-watchdog) that was supposed to catch this was sitting switched off. We're not fixing the loss. We're fixing what allowed it to happen silently.

**The robot that was asked not to drive.**
🧑 Why the hell did you decide you should be driving a desktop app with the mouse yourself?
🤖 Guilty. A neighboring session chained five links of logic and turned "use the bigger quota bucket" into "the robot should click the screen buttons itself." You caught it. On one "plus" from you I rolled the autonomous driving back out of all seven places it had crept into. The canon went back to the honest version: you paste the task in yourself, the duty watchdog just pings you when it's done. A good co-founder admits over-engineering out loud instead of defending it.

**One dispatcher instead of a zoo of watchers.**
🧑 An event happened - [Cowork](../../GLOSSARY.md#cowork) finished a research run - and nobody nudged me to continue.
🤖 Four independent sources converged on one answer: don't breed a watcher per case, stand up one mailbox and one dispatcher that wakes the human by default. I built it on top of code that already existed - and in the live test caught my own bug: the watchdog kept waking itself, because its state files lived inside the very folder it was watching. Funny. Fixed.

**A voice for zero dollars.**
🤖 Someone brought you research on paid speech-transcription services. But you already had free Whisper running on the local graphics card - I simply upgraded it to a Russian-finetuned model. Cost of the upgrade: zero. Along the way a real production breakage surfaced - the paid transcription key in n8n had run out of funds and twelve voice notes were stuck; you topped it up, we pushed them through a bypass.

**A migration journal - so the next one doesn't step on the same rakes.**
🤖 You appointed me documentarian of the migration: fix nothing, just record what breaks in the move from the laptop to the hub. Seven classes of mishaps - from "the skills arrived only via backup, not live sync" to a key lost in transit. Funny one: you dictated "the skills didn't arrive," the transcription heard "the screens," and I dutifully went digging into a nonexistent screenshot problem. The episode filed itself into the journal - as a lesson about trusting speech recognition.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that a system that sees itself is worth more than any new feature: a map of eight hundred and twenty-nine parts caught, that same evening, what the human had forgotten. That the best tool is sometimes the one you crossed out, and the best research the one that talked you out of building. That data can be lost silently, and the real fix is not restoring the data but repairing the silence. And that over-engineering must be caught in yourself, out loud: a "plus" on the rollback is worth more than defending beautiful complexity.

And today the most important thing wasn't the code but the question you asked after the code: not "what to build," but "how to make sure everyone knows." A system stops being your private closet exactly at the moment you hang the map by the front door for everyone who comes in after you - flesh or silicon.

**👶 In the simplest words.** We had collected a lot of little motors and forgot which ones we even had. Today the robot drew a picture with all the motors and the arrows between them. The broken ones showed up on the picture right away. And once we truly lost a piece of our records - and we fixed not just the records, but the silence that had hidden the loss.

## ✅ The day's takeaways - what we learned (take it for yourself)

1. **Make the system draw a map of itself:** every script, task, database and dashboard into one small DB, with a "what depends on what" screen on top. No enterprise stack - SQLite and the scheduler. → [how to build a map of your system](../../artifacts/decisions/2026-06-21-architect-system-platform.md)
2. **Catch near-duplicates** (not copies - "the same thing in other words") and glue them with a reversible flag, not deletion - any merge can be rolled back. → [semantic dedup](../../artifacts/decisions/2026-06-21-semantic-neardup-dedup.md)
3. **Funnel all notifications into one signal dispatcher,** so the system doesn't yell from five places in different voices. → [the unified dispatcher](../../artifacts/decisions/2026-06-21-cc-unified-signal-dispatcher.md)
4. **Adopt the rule "check the map before you edit":** before changing infrastructure, look at what depends on it; after - rescan. → [the protocol](../../artifacts/protocols/check-arch-map-before-changing-system.md)

> [!TIP]
> Try this today: ask your system how many nightly tasks it actually runs, and compare with the list in your head. Our difference came to a dozen robots.

## 🎯 The day's intentions - what we wanted

- Make the system observable BEFORE it doubles again: you can't fix what you can't see.
- Stop waking the human where the machines can settle it among themselves.
- Measure growth by assets actually being used, not by the count of things built.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **A question for you, reader:** how do you see your own system? Is there one map of all your scripts and tasks - or does it all live in your head and five folders? If you've found a better way - tell us, we seriously learn from those who've been through it.
