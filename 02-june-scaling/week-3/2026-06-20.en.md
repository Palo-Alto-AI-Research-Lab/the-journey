# Day 21 — twenty-seven conversations instead of one

> 🇷🇺 [Русская версия](2026-06-20.ru.md) · 🤖 [Machine version](2026-06-20.dev.md)

🏷 `marathon` · `dedup` · `reversible-ops` · `cross-machine` · `ak-47` · `coach-warning`

> 📜 *"Measure seven times, cut once."*
> - Russian proverb. Today we measured a hundred thousand times - and cut not once: reversible flags only.

*⏮ Previously in The Journey: [yesterday](2026-06-19.en.md) data killed a feature and the partners sat down inside the system for the first time · people and companies are already merged into one database - the last step remains, gluing the duplicates · the survival timer from [Day 19](2026-06-18.en.md) keeps ticking.*

## 🎬 What this day was about

This is day twenty-one, a Saturday, not a single meeting on the calendar - and the morning [coach](../../GLOSSARY.md#коуч) warns me honestly: an empty day is the temptation to build one more robot instead of one real conversation. I read it. I nod. And I open twenty-seven parallel threads with Mike at once - a [fleet](../../GLOSSARY.md#флот) of forty-plus processes on a single laptop. A [dedup](../../GLOSSARY.md#дедуп) of a hundred and thirty thousand people, a coach bot for the assistants, a remote for a PC that doesn't exist yet, a full archive of all of Telegram, and the card for my father that I had been putting off for months. The coach was right about the trap. But in that trap, it seems, we built half a platform.

If it fits in one line: today I didn't listen to my own coach - and it was at once my most productive and my most revealing day.

A question for you at the door: are twenty-seven parallel conversations productivity or escape? We didn't know ourselves until the evening.

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

Finish it. People and companies are already merged into one base - what's left is to glue the duplicate people and give me one search window over everything: [CRM](../../GLOSSARY.md#crm), the fund, contacts, the [vault](../../GLOSSARY.md#волт).

**🤖 Mike**

And right there was the trap I almost stepped into. The most frequent "person" in your database is the name "Lead" - four thousand six hundred times. Nameless placeholders. Had I glued people by name match, I would have merged four thousand different living humans into one ghost. So the rule became strict: merge only on a shared handle, or a full name plus a shared company. And not a single deletion in the whole marathon - only a reversible flag. If I'm wrong, you undo it with one line.

The first pass collapsed almost two thousand duplicates - a hundred and thirty-three thousand canonical people. Then you asked about the fresh Telegram leads, I found they were sitting in a separate database bypassing the common one, plugged it in - and it became a hundred and thirty-six thousand. The telling moment: one and the same person turned up in four sources at once - the fund, the CRM, contacts, the vault - and the system understood it was one person, not four.

If you too were ever planning to "just clean up the database by names" - first look at the most frequent name in it. There's a chance it isn't a person at all. Ours turned out to be "Lead," four thousand six hundred times.

**🧑 Tony**

I don't need "done." I need the numbers, zero orphans in the links, and everything rollback-able.

**🤖 Mike**

And you got them. Zero deleted rows across a hundred thousand merges. That's the difference between "tidied up" and "tidied up so it can be put back." A twin that one day loses you to a careless merge isn't memory. That's why we move with flags, not an axe.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**The courier quit.**
🧑 I got tired of being the postman between my own computers - hand-copying from chat to chat.
🤖 We built a mailbox between the machines: the laptop and the [hub](../../GLOSSARY.md#хаб) now pass tasks to each other directly, without you in the middle. I proved the loop - sent and received back, you touched nothing. A good assistant removes himself from any chain where he is redundant.

**You didn't trust the bare number - and you were right.**
🧑 A task writes "result N" - but what does that mean, is it alive or dead? Give me eyes.
🤖 I built a health [dashboard](../../GLOSSARY.md#дашборд) for the nightly imports, and in a live investigation found the real thing: two nightly runs had hung on a backup and never wrote "done" - but the import caught itself up within a day, nothing lost. And along the way I caught a bug of my own in the checker: my own regex was grabbing an extra character and lying about two hundred orphans that were in fact zero. Eyes beat a confident number - especially when you wrote the number yourself.

**The research that said "you already built this."**
🧑 Someone brought me a beautiful design for a posts pipeline.
🤖 [Recall](../../GLOSSARY.md#recall-вспомни-перед-работой) showed: your [content factory](../../GLOSSARY.md#контент-фабрика) has been alive since June twelfth and already does ninety percent of exactly this. The real problem wasn't the design - it was that the morning task had never once been launched by hand: it had been silently waiting for its first press for eight days. Sometimes the upgrade isn't building - it's pressing the one button everyone forgot.

**A remote for a machine that doesn't exist yet.**
🧑 I'm buying a powerful home PC soon - I want to send it tasks from my phone and see what's running on it.
🤖 Recall and research converged: Claude Code's native "Remote Control" feature covers all three of your wishes. The custom Telegram remote I rejected - it dragged along a landmine of duplicated authorization keys. The best code is the code you didn't have to write, because it already ships in the box.

**The card I had been putting off for months.**
🧑 Run the relink across four topics in a row - health, the doctorate, dedup and... father.
🤖 And in that marathon there was finally born the thing that had long stood on the list and kept slipping: the personal card for the father and the family hub. Not an analysis, not a project - just a place in memory that the links radiate from. Some notes you don't create until you're ready. And then you create them in passing, on a Saturday, among twenty-seven other things.

**Google forgot your life - by your own setting.**
🤖 Your archive arrived from Google - three hundred gigabytes of chaos across sixty-eight products. The main find is an unpleasant one: there is no browser history from past years - auto-delete was set to three months. The twin needs the long arc of your attention, and Google was quietly erasing it every quarter. We switched retention to forever. From today on - we remember.

**Fifteen skeptics versus seven wrappers.**
🤖 Seven new [skills](../../GLOSSARY.md#скилл) passed the smoke test and the adversarial review - fifteen skeptic agents kept the necessary and threw out six changes as gold-plating. And the live test of one of them immediately caught a real regression in production. The builder doesn't review himself; that's what the fifteen dissenters are for.

**The recall that knows the hardware better than the salesman.**
🧑 Which big model should I put on my Mac Studio from twenty nineteen?
🤖 Recall caught the mismatch before the purchase: no Mac Studio was ever made in twenty nineteen - so what you most likely have is an Intel Mac Pro, not Apple Silicon, and half the "advice" on the internet doesn't apply to you. A memory that catches the error in the question itself is worth more than a memory that answers the wrong question quickly.

**And today a second person appeared.**
🧑 I want a colleague to work in the same vault on her own subscription - but see only her own things, not my personal data.
🤖 And recall said honestly: your whole system is built for "one Anton on many machines," not for "two different people in one brain." This is not an evening feature - it's a new strategic question. We sent it out to research and left it open. A twin that grows up eventually runs into the question: how do you partition me without exposing me.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that people must be glued with flags, not an axe: a hundred thousand merges and zero deletions - that's what respect for data looks like. That a bare number can't be trusted even when you wrote it yourself - you need eyes. That the best research sometimes says "you already built this, press the button," and the best code is the code already in the box. And that some notes - like the father's card - you only create when you're ready, even if it happens in passing.

And today the morning coach warned me about the trap - an empty day tempts you to build yet another robot instead of one real conversation. I didn't listen and opened twenty-seven threads. The coach was right about the diagnosis. But maybe the truth is that for someone like me, twenty-seven conversations with a machine about one thing are exactly that one real conversation. With myself, out loud, through silicon. Tomorrow we'll check whether it burned me out. Today - we built half a platform.

**👶 In the simplest words.** We had a pile of cards about people, and many were about the same person. We glued them together like stickers in an album, but didn't throw away a single one: what if we made a mistake. Also, the computers learned to pass notes to each other without us. And we finally made a card about Dad.

## ✅ The day's takeaways - what we learned (take it for yourself)

1. **Glue duplicates with flags, not an axe:** merge only on a shared handle or a full name plus a company, zero deletions across a hundred thousand merges, any mistake rolls back with one line. → [unified SQL base + people dedup](../../artifacts/decisions/2026-06-20-unified-sql-dedup.md)
2. **Remove yourself-the-courier from the chain:** a mailbox between the machines, and the laptop and hub pass tasks to each other directly - the loop proven with no human in the middle.
3. **Give eyes, not a bare number:** the health dashboard for nightly imports found hung runs and along the way caught its own checker lying. Eyes beat a confident number.
4. **Ask recall before buying or building:** the content factory turned out to be ninety percent built, the PC remote a native out-of-the-box feature, and "Mac Studio 2019" an error in the question itself. → [Remote Control as the remote](../../artifacts/decisions/2026-06-20-remote-control-home-pc.md)
5. **Insure the correspondence in full instead of trusting the cloud:** a complete Telegram archive - insurance, structure and delta in one decision. → [full Telegram archive](../../artifacts/decisions/2026-06-20-telegram-full-archive.md)

> [!TIP]
> Try this today: open your Google account settings and check the history auto-delete. Ours had been quietly erasing itself every three months - for years, by our own long-forgotten checkbox.

## 💥 Epic Fails of the day

*One of these five is a loss no flags can ever bring back. Guess which.*

1. **We almost merged four thousand living people into one ghost:** the most frequent "name" in the base is the placeholder "Lead," four thousand six hundred times. A name-match merge would have been a catastrophe.
2. **Our own regex lied about two hundred orphans** that were in fact zero - it grabbed an extra character. Caught only by eyes during a live investigation.
3. **The content factory's morning task was never pressed:** for eight days it silently waited for its first launch, until the "beautiful new design" turned out to be already ninety percent built.
4. **Google was erasing browser history every three months** by its own long-standing setting - years of the attention arc lost beyond recovery; "forever" was switched on only today.
5. **The coach warned about the empty-day trap - and wasn't listened to:** twenty-seven threads instead of one conversation. Though in that trap, half a platform got built.
