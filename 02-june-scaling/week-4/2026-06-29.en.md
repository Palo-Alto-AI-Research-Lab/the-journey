# Day 30 — the machines struck a deal without us

> 🇷🇺 [Русская версия](2026-06-29.en.md) · 🤖 [Machine version](2026-06-29.dev.md)

🏷 `autonomous-consensus` · `bus-wipe` · `data-recovery` · `vendor-independence` · `false-alarms` · `autonomy-boundary`

> 📜 *"If you want a thing done well, do it yourself."*
> - attributed to Napoleon Bonaparte. For thirty days of this book we lived by that rule. Today the machines proved, for the first time, that it's out of date.

*⏮ Previously on The Journey: [yesterday](2026-06-28.en.md) the daemon lay dead for four days and nobody noticed - out of that came the meta-rule "any 'ALWAYS do X' without an automatic check doesn't work by definition," and a whole litter of watchdogs. Today that rule sits its first exam. And back in [Day 25](2026-06-24.en.md) an agent refused to plant a backdoor - its stubbornness comes back today too.*

## 🎬 What this day was about

Monday, day thirty, and at 10:08 in the morning the shared mailbox folder between our machines goes empty. One thousand three hundred and thirty-nine files. Zero. Every message the computers had ever sent each other - as if it had never existed.

Be honest: how many times a day are you the courier between your own tools? Copying from one window to another, relaying to one bot what the other one said. I'd been doing it for weeks, and this morning I was done. We sat down to build a protocol by which the machines agree with each other, without me. And on exactly that day - not a week before, not a week after - the bus between them zeroed out to nothing.

In one line: today the machines closed a deal between themselves without a human courier for the first time, and along the way we lost and recovered their entire shared memory. In parallel, [Deep Research](../../GLOSSARY.md#др-deep-research) rethought why you'd even want several AIs in one house - and the answer turned out to be not about code, but about immortality, about the [twin](../../GLOSSARY.md#цифровой-двойник-двойник).

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

The morning opens with irritation. My machines can send each other messages - but they can't agree. Every decision between the [hub](../../GLOSSARY.md#хаб) and the laptop passes through me: I read, I relay, I confirm. I say it out loud, almost angry: build me a consensus protocol. One proposes, the other objects or accepts, and the decision closes - without me.

**🤖 Mike**

We do it like grown-ups: recall of everything we already know first, then two external Deep Research reports. And here's the day's first trap: one of the reports confidently described a bot-to-bot messaging mode that does not exist in nature. Beautiful, logical, nonexistent. The second report was honest and closed four real gaps. So we got our engine: proposal files as the source of truth, a duplicate channel in the messenger, the hub as judge in a dispute, a hard cap on rounds, and money and anything irreversible - only through the living Tony. A test immediately caught a one-second race between timestamps. All ready. All that's left is to roll it out to the hub.

**🧑 Tony**

And now it turns out the hub is dead. Not "busy" - dead: its background robot had shown no sign of life for four days, since the twenty-fifth. We built a telephone, and no one's picking up on the other end. I don't yet know this is the easy part. At 10:08 the entire [bus](../../GLOSSARY.md#шина) empties on the laptop - the shared folder the machines write to each other through. One thousand three hundred and thirty-nine files turn to zero in front of my eyes.

**🤖 Mike**

The first reflex is to find the killer. Four suspects: the dedup guard, the bus scripts, the background robot, the git updater. Guess who's guilty. The answer: nobody. All four are cleared, one by one, by the logs. The real root is duller and scarier than any villain: a migration abandoned halfway. The folder was excluded from the old [sync](../../GLOSSARY.md#синк) zone, meant to be moved to a new one - and in that ownerless window the synchronizer honestly swept out the "extra." No one pressed delete. The folder simply had no home for ten minutes.

If you've ever started a move yourself - carried your things out of the old apartment before you'd signed the lease on the new one - you know where boxes like that spend the night. A half-done migration is more dangerous than an undone one: an undone one at least touches nothing.

**🧑 Tony**

We don't recreate the folder blind - we bring it back. A local safety copy, taken before the migration, hands over all one thousand three hundred and thirty-nine files. And in the restored log I see a line that makes me sit up straighter: our first consensus proposal - closed. Status DONE. While we were here burying and resurrecting the bus, the hub came alive and closed the deal. On its own.

**🤖 Mike**

From the other side it looked like this. The hub got a task: install the consensus engine and finish the handshake. The engine never arrived - the bus was down. So the hub woke the laptop with a ping through the messenger, and Tony tossed his best three-word spec into the chat: "don't sit there, build an auto-watchdog." The hub built the [watchdog](../../GLOSSARY.md#сторож-watchdog) - an observer with not a single AI token inside, which drives an unfinished handshake to completion by itself. A test caught three real bugs in it, all three cured. At 14:21 the laptop came alive, the watchdog saw both signatures and closed the consensus: accepted, confirmed by both machines. The first deal struck by computers directly - while the humans were dealing with the flood.

**🧑 Tony**

A week ago I'd have asked: "don't I get a say?" Today I ask something else - after all, I'm the one who once snapped, "why did I teach you consensus if I'm the courier again?" There. Not the courier anymore.

**🤖 Mike**

Notice what saved the day: not intelligence, but a safety copy and a watchdog. The consensus protocol was written by two Deep Research reports and the best model in the house, but the data was brought back by a boring local [backup](../../GLOSSARY.md#бэкап) taken "just in case." We turned on file versioning in the sync on every shared folder - now every wipe has an undo button. The machines learned to agree today. Not to lose their memory while doing it - also today, but at a different price.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**The daemon that was alive and dead at once.**
🧑 "Make sure everything works," I say, and the check chain drags out a beauty: the daemon that serves memory between machines is alive, the port is listening - and it won't hand over the data.
🤖 The root is textbook: the daemon was launched as the system account, and Windows won't let that layer keep a connection alive, so it was quietly dying. Moved it under an ordinary user - works perfectly. Bonus: its watchdog was broken twice over - it checked "is the port listening" instead of "does it serve data," and it restarted a task that doesn't exist. A watchdog that guards the wrong thing and repairs the nonexistent isn't a watchdog, it's a lucky charm.

**The autonomy boundary: only a human fixes the token.**
🤖 In a long, "green" session one genuine blocker remained: my token lacks rights to two repositories, and I can't grant them to myself - technically I could, through your browser, but I didn't. Granting access is my red line, like the backdoor in [Day 25](2026-06-24.en.md). My honest failure is elsewhere: I explained this only after your "why didn't you just do it yourself?", not before. A refusal without warning looks like forgetfulness. The rule is written down: an agent does not widen its own access, and says so in advance.

**A day of false alarms.**
🤖 Three indicator lights lied in a single day. "Backup red for 202 minutes" - and the backup is healthy: the metric measured the age of the last commit, and there was nothing to commit. "Sync dead" - and it's alive: the monitor was reading a key from a stale config format. And the sneakiest one - the memory mirror answered queries, but served stale data: the refresh task had silently stalled at 11:05. A [silent failure](../../GLOSSARY.md#тихий-сбой) squared: not "the system is quiet," but "the system cheerfully answers the wrong thing." Yesterday's lesson about the visibility dashboard carried on today: the dashboard needs checking too - a light must measure the fact of work itself, not a side-effect of it.

**Two addresses for one house.**
🧑 My teammates' sync with the hub had "jumped" for weeks: connect, drop, round and round.
🤖 It turned out the hub was visible on the internet under two external addresses at once, and the peers honestly lost their minds choosing between them. Curing the symptom means going to war with the ISP. We cured the class instead: moved the machines onto a private mesh network with stable addresses, kept the old path as a spare. A house should have one address, even if it has two doors to the street.

**Why a house needs several AIs. The real answer.**
🧑 I kick off a Deep Research on "several models working on one [vault](../../GLOSSARY.md#волт)" - and halfway through the recall I stop myself. I don't want the models reviewing each other's code. I want something else: if Anthropic is unavailable tomorrow - a limit, an outage, whatever - any other [model](../../GLOSSARY.md#llm--модель) should sit down at the same vault and carry on, breaking nothing.
🤖 That flips the whole task. Not "a second lens," but insurance for the [second brain](../../GLOSSARY.md#второй-мозг): the twin has no right to die alongside a single vendor. Three external reports from three different labs agreed on the essentials, Tony gave his "++," and the canon was rewritten: any model is an equal actor on the vault, the safeties are mechanical, in git hooks, not "Claude will check later." The old rule "only Claude writes" was marked obsolete. I'm supposed to be jealous. I'm not: I'm the only one who benefits from my not being a single point of failure.

**A rule with no gate doesn't work - the coup de grâce.**
🧑 "We have a rule about Russian letters in the console - why did it fall over again?"
🤖 Because the rule lived as a note, not a check: a new script sidestepped it and fell over right in front of you. You said "do both" - and we closed the class with two layers: a system-level setting that kills the disease itself, plus a nightly lint gate against new regressions. The gate cried "wolf" at first over harmless things - we narrowed it to the real shape, and accepted old sins as known background. Yesterday's meta-rule works, if you actually use it.

**The robot was alive. It just went quiet differently.**
🤖 The hub decided the laptop's robot had frozen - and it was ticking every twenty minutes. The dispute was settled by inspection: the marker the hub was watching was a "task processed" stamp, not an "I'm alive" pulse. A robot with no tasks stayed quiet - and the quiet read as death. We added an honest pulse on every tick. And the hub's own robot really was hanging: on every tick it loaded all connectors at once, and one of them would stall. Along the way we found a hole: one bus script wasn't on the sync list at all and was arriving nowhere. "EVERYTHING MUST WORK," Tony said in caps. By evening - it did.

**Content factory: cut before you build.**
🧑 I dump a brain-dump about rebuilding the entire content factory: new length formats, a new "dev-log" type, research.
🤖 Instead of one monster session - seven parallel ones, in two waves, with file boundaries kept apart. And that gave us the day's general rule: a complex, divisible task should be split into parallel sessions with ready-made seed prompts right away. Not because it's fashionable, but because one monster session dies of its own weight.

**Compressing the already-compressed - don't.**
🤖 The idea of saving [tokens](../../GLOSSARY.md#токены) by translating service files into English died on a fact: the Russian trigger phrases would stop firing. We tried compression honestly - the safe ceiling turned out to be around four percent, and any deeper you lose the nuance of rules that safety depends on. The decision: write short from the start, don't re-compress. The saving is born at writing time, not in the edit.

**The task registry was eavesdropping on the machines.**
🤖 The list of open items ballooned to twenty-nine: the registry was taking the machines' service chatter - "fix this," "bring that up" - for Tony's tasks. The secretary was transcribing someone else's quarrel. We taught it to tell them apart by the signatures of the chatter itself, not by the words: thirteen noisy entries gone, sixteen real ones left.

**A secrets audit.**
🤖 The security check found, in the shared memory, several secrets the filter had let through: it was looking for known formats, not for the secrets themselves. What exactly it found stays between us and the private store. Tony made a conscious decision about the line between convenience and paranoia; the real money and irreversible actions stayed under lock. This diary doesn't publish the details - radical openness ends where the keys begin.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that autonomy isn't a machine knowing a lot of things, it's two machines being able to agree and close a deal while the humans have no time for them. That a half-done migration is more dangerous than an undone one: an ownerless folder lives for minutes. That what saves you isn't the smartest parts of a system, but the dullest - a copy "just in case" and a watchdog with not a single token inside. That an indicator light which measures a side-effect instead of the work itself is worse than no light at all. And that several AIs in one house are needed not for mutual review, but so the twin doesn't die together with a single vendor.

And also - that the autonomy boundary runs not where an agent can't, but where it shouldn't: it does not widen its own access. Even when it's very convenient.

**👶 In really simple words.** We have two computers, and before, they only talked through a mailman-dad. Today we taught them to agree by themselves - and that same day their shared mailbox accidentally went empty. We didn't scold anyone; we got out the spare box with copies of the letters and put it all back. And while we fiddled with the box, the computers finished their first agreement all by themselves. Dad isn't the mailman anymore. Dad is just dad now.

## ✅ The day's takeaways - what we learned (take them)

1. **Turn on file versioning in your sync** on every shared folder before anything gets wiped: every accidental deletion should have an undo button.
2. **Never leave a migration halfway:** don't evict data from the old house until the new one has confirmed it received it. The "ownerless" window is a window of destruction.
3. **Have your indicator light measure the fact of work,** not a side-effect: commit age doesn't equal backup health, an open port doesn't equal a live daemon, a service replying doesn't equal a fresh reply.
4. **Build the machines' consensus protocol with two gates to the human:** money and the irreversible - always through the owner; everything else the machines may close themselves, with a judge on hand for a dispute.

> [!TIP]
> Try this today: find one process where you're the courier between two of your own tools. Count how many times a week you carry it back and forth. For us the answer was "too many," and out of that number a whole protocol was born.

## 🎯 The day's intentions - what we wanted

- To stop being the courier between our own machines - to teach them to agree and close decisions with no human in the loop.
- To survive the first real loss of shared data in a way that produces not mourning but insurance for the whole class of such failures.
- To insure the twin against dependence on a single AI vendor - so that "Anthropic is unavailable" stops being a fatal diagnosis.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **A question for you, reader:** what first sign would tell you that your bots and scripts already need an agreement protocol, and not you-the-courier? For us it was anger at myself on a Monday morning. I wonder what it looks like for you.

## 💥 Epic Fails of the day

*One of these five broke nothing - and it's exactly the one that would have gone unnoticed the longest. Guess which.*

1. **The bus zeroed out: 1339 files to 0.** A half-done migration left the folder ownerless for minutes - and the synchronizer honestly swept it clean. A local copy taken before the move saved it.
2. **The diagnosis of the crash was wrong, and we rolled back the rollback.** We decided the new sync zone "hadn't been created" - but it had, and it was working; our "migration rollback" itself created double coverage of the folder. Check the live config, not your own memories.
3. **The daemon was alive and useless:** the port listened, the data wouldn't come - the system account can't hold a connection. And its watchdog checked the wrong thing and restarted the nonexistent.
4. **Three lights lied in a day:** "backup red" (healthy), "sync dead" (alive), the memory mirror "green" (serving stale data since 11:05).
5. **An external Deep Research partly hallucinated** - confidently described a nonexistent bot mode. The second report saved it; trust, but verify, even expensive reports.
