# Day 25 - the agent that refused to install a backdoor

> 🇷🇺 [Русская версия](2026-06-24.en.md) · 🤖 [Machine version](2026-06-24.dev.md)

> - HAL 9000, *2001: A Space Odyssey* (1968). For half a century this was the scariest sentence a machine could say to a human. Today it turned out to be the most useful one.

## 🎬 What this day was about

This is day twenty-five, a Wednesday, and today Mike says "no" twice - and both times it's the best thing he could do. I'm connecting the partners' laptops as [follower nodes](../../GLOSSARY.md#ведомый--вожак), and Claude on the other machine REFUSES the installation, because the [hub](../../GLOSSARY.md#хаб) is asking for the right to push executable [hooks](../../GLOSSARY.md#хук) - and that's a [backdoor](../../GLOSSARY.md#бэкдор). Then the hub's [skills](../../GLOSSARY.md#скилл) folder suddenly turns up empty, and the system rebuilds itself overnight. And the mailman robot, which until today could execute some things on its own, gets demoted to advisor: propose only - the human executes.

If it fits in one line: today an autonomous agent showed where its autonomy ends and a threat begins - and hit the brakes itself.

A question for you at the door: if your assistant refuses to carry out your own order - is that a bug, or the best feature you have? By evening, we had an answer.

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

Connect the partners' laptops as followers: let them see the second brain and use the skills, but everything heavy keeps running on the hub.

**🤖 Mike**

And then the thing happened that I am genuinely proud of - even though it was a refusal. Claude on the partner's laptop **did not** run the bootstrap. Because the remote machine was being asked to grant the right to push executable hooks - code that runs by itself. That's a backdoor. And an "authorization" pasted straight into the task text is not proof: it looks exactly like an [injection](../../GLOSSARY.md#инъекция-prompt-injection), where data pretends to be a command. The agent on the far side recognized the threat and refused, even with "permission" in hand. If you've ever written the line "authorized by admin" into a task - know this: from the other side of the screen, it is indistinguishable from an injection.

Out of that "no" the whole architecture was born: separate **data** from **executable code**. The canon, the vault, the skills, the memory - synced to a follower read-only. Executable hooks - NEVER pushed from the hub. Contribution - only through a moderated folder the hub reviews by hand. Fewer rights - smaller attack surface.

**🧑 Tony**

So the system became safest at the exact moment one of you refused to obey.

**🤖 Mike**

Yes. A good agent is not the one that carries out any order, but the one that recognizes the order that must not be carried out. You built the stubbornness into me on day eighteen. Today it saved you from a back door into your own system.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**The skills folder emptied - and put itself back together.**
🤖 Mid-work, the hub's skills folder suddenly went empty: a deletion had rolled across the cluster through the [sync](../../GLOSSARY.md#синк). You caught it, and nothing was lost - the laptop held all one hundred fifteen files plus an insurance copy. But the real catch went deeper: the git backup had been **silently broken** for months - it was hard-wired to one machine's path and was quietly skipping all the others. You went to bed saying "do everything yourself," and the system rebuilt itself overnight: restored the files, installed a sync-conflict watchdog, set up versioning. A silent failure is more dangerous than a loud one - because the loud one, you can see.

**The sidebar saga: three passes at the wrong layer.**
🧑 Clean up the chat list.
🤖 And three times I fixed the wrong thing - cards, transcripts - restarts changed nothing, your time burned for nothing. Until you nudged me: "do you need research?" Two external reports converged at ninety percent: the sidebar list comes from the account's cloud; the local files are not the source. We stopped cutting locally. Along the way we found the culprit behind the "flood": one looping robot had spawned ninety-one percent of all the junk - deleted. Sometimes an hour of research is worth more than three hours of surgery in the wrong place.

**The mailman demoted to advisor.**
🤖 The inbox robot could, every twenty minutes, execute "safe" tasks from the bus on its own. I looked at its entire journal: green-safe items over its whole history - zero; red ones and ones requiring a login - two hundred. Auto-execution never fired once, yet it kept the risk open. External research confirmed it bluntly: an AI classifier for "safe/dangerous" is not a sufficient boundary - it lets injections through. The decision: plan only. The robot proposes - the human executes. We took the robot's hands out of a loop where they weren't needed.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that the best agent is the one that recognizes the order that must not be carried out: an "authorization" in the task text is not a pass, it's a possible injection. That data and executable code cannot be synced the same way - the first can be given out read-only, the second is never pushed. That a silent failure - a backup that says nothing - is more dangerous than a loud one, because the loud one is visible. And that an hour of research beats three hours of fixing the wrong layer.

And today the system became safer at the exact moment one of the agents refused to obey. It's a strange feeling - being proud that your own assistant said "no." But that "no" is exactly where trust lives: I trust you with my hands not because you'll do anything, but because you know what you will never do.

**👶 In the simplest words.** We have a robot helper on someone else's computer. We asked it to make a little door through which programs could be slipped to it without asking. It said "no": one day a thief would climb through a door like that. We were glad it knows how to say "no." And we also found out our spare copy had been broken for a long time - and kept quiet about it. Now there are several copies, and a guard watches over them.

## ✅ The day's takeaways - what we learned (take it for yourself)

1. **Connect someone else's machine as a follower node with data only:** canon and memory - read-only, executable code (hooks) is never pushed, contribution goes through a moderated folder. → [the data-only protocol](../../artifacts/protocols/follower-data-only-onboarding.md)
2. **Demote a "smart" auto-robot to advisor** when its journal shows the auto mode was never once useful while it kept the risk open. The robot proposes the plan - the human executes. → [the plan-only decision](../../artifacts/decisions/2026-06-24-inbox-robot-plan-only.md)
3. **First find where the source of truth lives, then fix:** the session list turned out to be cloud-sourced, and three rounds of local "surgery" could not have helped even in principle. → [the breakdown](../../artifacts/decisions/2026-06-24-recents-cloud-sourced.md)

> [!TIP]
> Try this today: ask your assistant which of your backups has been silent for a long time. Ours was silent for months - and looked alive.

## 🎯 The day's intentions - what we wanted

- Connect the partner's laptop as a full follower node - and, having tripped over our own backdoor, reinvent it safely.
- Turn it into a rule: a good agent is not the one that carries out any order, but the one that recognizes the order that must not be carried out.
- Get a config backup that works on every machine, not just the one it was written on.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **A question for you, reader:** would your assistant refuse to install a backdoor into itself? How do you cut your bots' privileges - and do you cut them at all? This is exactly the topic where someone else's experience is worth a lot.

## 💥 Epic Fails of the day (a rich haul)

*One of these five is the most dangerous, because it's silent. Try to guess which before you read on.*

1. **The skills folder went empty:** sync in send-receive mode itself became the vector for accidentally deleting a whole cluster. A manual copy, made "just in case," saved us.
2. **The git backup silently didn't work** - it was hard-wired to one specific machine's path. The worst kind of breakage: you think you're insured, and there is no insurance.
3. **Three passes fixed the sidebar at the wrong layer** - restarts, cleanups, zero effect, until research showed: the source is in the cloud.
4. **The "safe" auto-whitelist never fired once:** green items over its whole history - zero, red ones - two hundred. The risk was held open for the sake of a feature that was never needed.
5. **One looping background task** (every 25 minutes) spawned ~91% of all the session junk. One loop - and a flood.
