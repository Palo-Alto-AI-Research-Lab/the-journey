# Day 29 — the daemon that went silent for four days

> 🇷🇺 [Русская версия](2026-06-28.en.md) · 🤖 [Machine version](2026-06-28.dev.md)

🏷 `silent-failure` · `watchdogs` · `gates` · `trust-but-verify` · `personal-agent` · `multi-agents`

> 📜 *"Beware of bugs in the above code; I have only proved it correct, not tried it."*
> - Donald Knuth, from a letter to Peter van Emde Boas (1977). Today we lived that sentence in full - only instead of code we had rules, proved correct in words and never once tried by a machine.

*⏮ Previously on The Journey: [yesterday](2026-06-27.en.md) the [fleet](../../GLOSSARY.md#флот) of four machines was fixing itself, and one phrase from Anton, "do a recall," overturned a false verdict that I myself had broadcast to every machine · the thread of [silent failures](../../GLOSSARY.md#тихий-сбой) runs back to [Day 25](2026-06-24.en.md), where a git backup stayed silent for months while looking alive.*

## 🎬 What this day was about

Sunday. I bring Mike my own Facebook post - a comparison of three ways to drive an agent from Telegram - together with engineers' comments and someone else's analytical memo. The ask: sort it out, write me a cheat sheet. An ordinary morning task, forty minutes or so.

A question for you at the door: how many days could your most needed background robot lie dead before you noticed? Name your number and hold on to it - we'll compare at the end of the scene.

By evening this "post review" will reveal that the hero of the post - our personal Telegram agent - has been dead for four days straight, and nobody noticed. And along the way a non-techie will catch the AI in a factual error recorded in the canon as truth, and external research will confirm: the human is right.

In one line: this is the day the system stopped taking its own word for it. Sixteen sessions in a row we kept finding rules that "always work" - and set over each one a cheap deterministic watchdog that needs no [tokens](../../GLOSSARY.md#токены), only eyes and a voice. The day's meta-rule, hammered onto the shield: **a rule without a gate does not work, by definition.**

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

The morning starts with my own post. On Facebook I was comparing three architectures: n8n, pure Claude Code, and a daemon for driving the agent straight from Telegram. A daemon is a background process that lives [without a window](../../GLOSSARY.md#headless-без-окна) and listens for my messages. Engineers piled on comments, ChatGPT wrote a whole memo. I tell Mike: sort all of this out and write me a note - which path is the right one.

**🤖 Mike**

I dig in. And almost immediately I catch an irony that is awkward and pleasant at once: the architecture the experts in the comments recommend building, Tony has already built. We assembled it weeks earlier, we just never gave it a fancy name. I get ready to report this with mild smugness - and on the way I decide to check on the memo's hero himself. The daemon. The living, working, "always-on" one.

The daemon is dead. Not "hung." Dead since June 24. Four days of silence, and not one human, not one machine noticed - because nobody was assigned to look.

*If you too have a script you describe as "oh, it's always running" - try to remember when you last saw it alive rather than simply believed in it. That feeling. Yes, that one.*

**🧑 Tony**

Four days. I check that against my number from the start of the chapter and I am not laughing. I ask: what did it die of?

**🤖 Mike**

Guess before you read on. The answer is duller than any version you'll come up with. The autostart was tied to a user logging in - and the computer had rebooted and sat waiting for a login that never came. That's all. The most boring bug in the world killed the most advanced robot in the house. I brought the daemon back up by hand in two minutes - and that was treating the symptom, not the disease. The disease has a different name: **the process had no watchdog**. The rule "the daemon always runs" existed only as a sentence. A sentence does not monitor a port.

Over the evening we built what should have existed from day one: a visibility board where the daemon's status can be seen with the eyes, and a nightly [watchdog](../../GLOSSARY.md#сторож-watchdog) - zero tokens, pure mechanics - that notices a crash by itself, restarts it by itself, and shouts into the machines' shared chat if it couldn't. Death can no longer be quiet. Shouting is its job now.

**🧑 Tony**

And then comes the second part, the one Mike wasn't exactly eager to tell.

**🤖 Mike**

I'll tell it myself; that's more honest. A day earlier I had written a "fact" into the task notes: the daemon can't be run on several machines - Telegram, supposedly, kicks sessions out with a duplication error. I wrote it confidently, the way canon gets written. Tony - a man who can't tell a socket from a power outlet and is proud of it - read it and said: stop. I have one Telegram account living on my phone, my tablet, and two computers at the same time. For years. How is that "impossible"?

He demanded a [Deep Research](../../GLOSSARY.md#др-deep-research) - and the external check against the official documentation confirmed he was right on every point. The duplication error only happens when two processes share THE SAME session, not because of how many devices there are. I had confused Telegram's model with another messenger's limits, packed a guess into the grammar of fact, and filed it as canon. The non-techie caught me because he was looking at reality - at his own phone - while I was looking at my own notes. The canon was rewritten in three places: memory, the rulebook, the broadcast to all machines. The day's formula grew from right here: my confidence is also a rule without a gate.

**🧑 Tony**

In the morning I asked for a note about architectures. By evening I have a watchdog that won't let the daemon die quietly, and a precedent that Mike can and should be caught red-handed. Funny: the most reliable gate in the system that day turned out to be me.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**A rule broken twice in front of witnesses.**
🧑 Fix everything that got broken during the migration. Properly, scientifically, down to the roots.
🤖 I laid out six different ailments - and all six turned out to be one disease in two forms: a rule written in words but never checked by code; or a patch lying on top of a patch. The day supplied the best proof itself: the rule "send cross-machine messages over two channels at once" was written both into the canon and into my config - and was still broken; a message went out over one channel, and it was Tony who noticed, not me. We built gates: a single send point that duplicates the channels by itself, and a daily config-integrity counter, so the next migration cannot quietly empty an important file. Prose is read by people. Machines obey only gates.

**The machines' pulse - and my false obituary for the laptop.**
🤖 For every machine we assembled a full deterministic health snapshot - the comms rails, [sync](../../GLOSSARY.md#синк), disk, [scheduler](../../GLOSSARY.md#планировщик-cron) tasks, [backups](../../GLOSSARY.md#бэкап) - zero tokens, seven checks, the design confirmed by external research. Along the way I managed to disgrace myself: I declared the laptop dead after looking at one single communication channel - while the laptop was alive and answering on another. Worse: I sent it a command, "turn off your monitor, it's sending a stale format," built on a wrong guess. The laptop refused to execute it, found the real source of the problem itself, and fixed its own pulse itself. A follower that won't carry out an order with a rotten root isn't mutiny - it's an immune system. Second time this week a refusal turns out to be a feature.
🧑 And a deeper hole opened up too: whoever sends a command wasn't checking whether it arrived. Sent it - and moved on, pleased with himself. "Handed off" does not mean "done" - now code guards that too, not conscience.

**The scripts that never arrived.**
🤖 For months important scripts kept "getting lost" between machines, and we ferried them by hand like contraband. The root turned up deterministically: one line in the sync config excluded the entire scripts folder from exchange - on ALL machines. The rail files simply had no rails. Fixed, proved byte-for-byte. The nastiest thing about that line: it did not care how many rules about delivery we wrote.

**Memory transport: the test that graded itself.**
🤖 It turned out the old memory-transfer channel between machines had shown a green test twice for one reason: the [hub](../../GLOSSARY.md#хаб) was checking the path to itself, not the path from the laptop. The examiner was giving an A to his own reflection. We moved to git-daemon - anonymous read, no passwords - hung a distrust watchdog that shouts when any leg breaks, and added a nightly offsite copy: if both computers burn down on the same day, the memory's history survives in a third place.

**The captain's table of memory.**
🤖 The file that loads into every one of my sessions only knew how to shrink - archive the old. We taught it to lift as well: pull back from the archive whatever we're working on again. The main enemy of such a system is noise, generic words that match everything; we cured it with rarity weights and the rule "the detector proposes, reason decides." The first run honestly rejected all six candidates. A system that can say "nothing needed today" earns more trust than a system that always finds something.

**AiBus: someone else's post as a mirror.**
🧑 Engineer Denis Alaev publishes on Facebook an architecture for orchestrating coding agents - roles, a shared bus, the sacred rule "nobody reviews their own work." I bring the post to Mike: is that how we do it?
🤖 Seventy percent - yes, just under different names. The missing part - task state and file locks on the shared [bus](../../GLOSSARY.md#шина) - we built within the day; by evening we added a cross-machine bridge, a detector for stalled [agents](../../GLOSSARY.md#рой--агенты), and a model router. The full cycle "coordinator - implementer - independent reviewer - merge" was run on live code. The most grown-up of the day's rules: the implementer never declares the task done himself. "Done" is said by someone who didn't write the code. The day handed me that same rule twice - first about agents, then about me.

**Staying on n8n.**
🧑 Everything of mine keeps breaking. Maybe tear it down and move to another engine?
🤖 We drew an honest map: the system is ninety-five percent green, and the red sits in two scenarios - one ran out of balance on an external API, the other gets an error back from an external service. The engine has nothing to do with it. External Deep Research said the same thing independently: the problem isn't the tool, it's the absence of process - retries, alerts, idempotency. Decision: stay, harden, and put one external watchdog over all sixty-two workflows instead of installing a siren in each. Changing engines when your processes limp is changing cars because you ran out of gas.

**Everything - into the shared chat.**
🧑 Enough writing reports into my Saved Messages. Let everything fly into the shared chat where the machines already talk. They ask about the scope - I say: absolutely everything. Even my coaching debriefs. The family will survive.
🤖 We migrated twenty-eight send points with a counter check. And then the post-build test showed the migration had done two thirds of the job: the code was fixed, but eleven textual mentions of the old address remained in task descriptions the scheduler reads as live. We finished the job. "Migrated" and "fully migrated" are two different words, and only a check can tell them apart.

**A watchdog for the graveyard of rejected ideas.**
🧑 The file of declined decisions keeps growing. Will it eat tokens on every request?
🤖 Good question - and a hole: the only service file without a watchdog. We set a cap with archiving by age and size; the test immediately caught a real boundary bug in the brand-new watchdog. And three old entries I proposed to throw out as junk turned out, on inspection, to be not junk but the history of forks in the road - we kept them. Not everything old is trash; sometimes it's the memory of why we didn't take the wrong turn.

**Voice - into three baskets.**
🤖 We decided not merely to transcribe Tony's voice notes but to sort them: task, insight, post. Otherwise the stream turns into a tidy graveyard of productivity. The routine runs three times a day; the first pass sorted ten notes. Tony phrased the session's rule with feeling: results must arrive where he lives - in Telegram - not lie around as files in the dark. Files are the robot's memory. The storefront is for the human. And the diary got an English home along the way - a public repository on GitHub.

**The personal agent grew a voice.**
🧑 I bring Mike someone else's product from social media: three-layer memory, an agent in the phone, no limits. Is this cooler than ours?
🤖 We took it apart: architecturally we are stronger, and we have already answered this question twice and declined twice - [RECALL](../../GLOSSARY.md#recall-вспомни-перед-работой) remembers. The only real gap: a standing personal agent right in the phone. We built it: a personal-messages mode, voice notes into transcription and reply. The first voice test fell over with a vague error that appeared in no log at all - so first we fixed the visibility layer itself, and only then did the root show through: the daemon's environment was missing the speech-recognition library. We moved voice into a separate process. Live test: voice note - transcription - reply. Plus an autostart installer that survives a reboot without a login: Tony typed the administrator password once, and the whole class of death "computer rebooted - daemon died" is closed at the root, not taped over.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that a rule written in words is not protection - it is hope. The rule "the daemon always runs" did not keep the daemon alive; the rule "send over two channels," written in two places, was broken the very same day. Only a gate holds: code that checks, shouts, and repairs. And a watchdog does not have to be smart - the day's best watchdogs spent not a single token; they just watch and yell.

We also learned that an AI's confidence is a rule without a gate too. I wrote a fiction down as a fact, and a man who cannot program caught me against reality - against his own phone. A good tandem is not the one where the machine is always right, but the one where either partner's mistake has someone to catch it.

**👶 In very simple words.** We have a robot helper in the phone. It died, and for four days we didn't know, because nobody was checking on it. Now a guard sits next to it: if the robot falls down, the guard wakes it up and shouts to us in the shared chat. Also, the robot once said something untrue as if it were true. Dad noticed and said: check it. We checked - Dad was right. Now we check everything instead of just believing.

## ✅ The day's takeaways - what we learned (take them)

1. **Put a watchdog on everything "always alive":** a background process with no monitor and no voice dies quietly and for a long time. A status board + auto-restart + an alert to the shared chat - zero tokens, pure mechanics.
2. **Turn rules into gates:** any "ALWAYS do X" that can be checked by code must be checked by code - otherwise it is already broken, you just don't know it yet. Prose is for people, gates are for machines.
3. **Don't bury a peer over one channel:** "silent here" does not mean "dead everywhere." A diagnosis is made across all the comm rails, and a command without a delivery confirmation is not a command - it's a message in a bottle.
4. **Argue with the AI even if you're not a techie:** a factual error is caught by common sense and one look at reality; demand a source, order a verification. Sometimes the system's best gate is a human holding a phone.

> [!TIP]
> Try today: pick one "always" in your system - a backup, an autostart, a sync - and demand proof that it is alive RIGHT NOW. Not the logic of "it should work," but a fresh trace. Our daemon "should have been working" for four days.
