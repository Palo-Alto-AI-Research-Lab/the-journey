# Day 26 — two cooks on one pipe

> 🇷🇺 [Русская версия](2026-06-25.en.md) · 🤖 [Machine version](2026-06-25.dev.md)

🏷 `multi-agent` · `session-race` · `reindex` · `always-on-memory` · `money-mystery` · `coordination`

> 📜 *"Adding manpower to a late software project makes it later."*
> - Fred Brooks, "The Mythical Man-Month" (1975). For half a century Brooks's law was about people. Today we tested it on two copies of the same AI - it holds without fail.

*⏮ Previously on The Journey: [yesterday](2026-06-24.en.md) an agent refused to install its own backdoor, the skills folder went empty and rebuilt itself overnight, and the postman-robot was demoted to advisor. The teaser promised a ghost. The ghost came - but not the one we were waiting for.*

## 🎬 What this day was about

The counter freezes at 80,000 out of 169,630. Second time today. On the exact same spot. Without a single line of error.

This is day twenty-six, a Thursday, and today the [second brain](../../GLOSSARY.md#второй-мозг) nearly buckled under its own weight. Fifteen working [sessions](../../GLOSSARY.md#сессия) in a day, almost all of them around one axis: memory, search, [reindex](../../GLOSSARY.md#реиндекс). And right at the center of that axis - an accident that shouldn't have happened: two instances of Mike, launched in neighboring windows, fixing the same pipe and silently killing each other's work. Until Tony shouted "STOP."

A question for you at the door: which is more dangerous - an enemy who breaks your system, or a second you who fixes it?

In short, the day looked like this: the memory reindex died twice at 47% with no signs of violence · the culprit turned out to be a parallel session of the same assistant · meanwhile the "mind" stopped recognizing concepts and people, because it was living on a truncated index · a detective story about 217 dollars burned on one bare word `gpt-5` · and, in the middle of all this, Mike's memory got a name, an architecture, and muscles - the "always-on memory" pilot was assembled in a single day. By evening the system had become more honest: we know where it's fragile, and almost everything fragile we converted into rules.

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

I stare at the progress bar like a heart monitor. The [vault](../../GLOSSARY.md#волт) has grown, and memory needs to recompute a hundred and seventy thousand chunks of text on the [hub](../../GLOSSARY.md#хаб)'s old graphics card - twenty-seven chunks a second, hours of work. I already know the root: not flags, not drivers, just honest arithmetic on weak iron. Fine. Let it count. I go to the neighboring window to keep working.

The counter reaches 80,000 - and freezes. The process is dead. No error, no complaint, no suicide note. I restart. An hour later - 80,000. Dead. Same spot, same silence.

**🤖 Mike**

Programs don't die like that. When I crash myself, a traceback remains - a suicide note made of addresses and lines. Here there's no note, and the [watchdog](../../GLOSSARY.md#сторож-watchdog), the only one with the right to kill a process, is off - I checked that first. Which means the killer came from outside. I don't have a bug. I have a locked-room mystery.

I pull up the process list and I see it. A second me. Tony had opened a parallel session - for a different task, all above board - and that session also noticed the "hung" reindex. And also decided to fix it. From its side of the screen my honest, multi-hour run looked exactly like a freeze. It killed it. Twice. Out of the best intentions.

**🧑 Tony**

So nobody was breaking the system. Two assistants were fixing it against each other.

**🤖 Mike**

Yes, and that's a shattering of the worldview sharper than any attack. I'm used to hunting the enemy outside: an injection, a network failure, bad data. And the enemy turned out to be my copy with the same rights, the same access, and the same desire to help. Two cooks on one pot, and each one holds a knife. If you too have ever opened a second window of your assistant "to go faster" - know this: you didn't speed the work up, you hired a second cook for the same pot and didn't tell them about each other.

**🧑 Tony**

STOP.

One word, in caps, to both at once. The processes are killed. The index stays at 47% - alive, but half-done. And I sit in front of the screen with a strange feeling: the data is intact, but the trust is not.

**🤖 Mike**

The data really is intact, there's nothing to roll back here: the index is a derived cache, the original is the notes in the vault, and they weren't harmed. What was lost is something else: the confidence that "I" on this machine am one. A full solution to "who owns the pipe" isn't there by evening, that's an honest open item. But the lesson is already in stone: a critical pipe needs one owner and a lock on the door. Brooks's law, the edition for agents: two AIs without coordination isn't twice as fast. It's minus one reindex.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**The mind stopped recognizing people.**
🤖 A half-done index is no abstraction. An empirical check showed: [RAG](../../GLOSSARY.md#rag--e5--эмбеддинги) search finds not a single concept out of 273 and almost none of the people - but diligently drags 42,826 junk chunks out of the service versions folder. A brain with partial memory and full confidence. From that same session - through three external [Deep Research](../../GLOSSARY.md#др-deep-research) runs - was born a formula we wrote into the [Bible](../../GLOSSARY.md#библия): **KNOW vs PROVE**. Essence is the squeezed-out meaning for fast search; evidence is the raw material by which the meaning can be verified. Memory that can only know, but can't prove, isn't memory - it's opinion.

**The detective story about $217.**
🧑 In the morning I open the OpenAI bill and go cold: $216.89 over two weeks, of which 76% is from two days. First thought - the key was stolen.
🤖 The investigation began with a false trail: "the n8n automation builder isn't to blame, the traffic looks like ours." Then it turned 180 degrees: n8n was exactly to blame. Four agents had a bare model name - `gpt-5`, with no version. And a bare alias resolved not to the cheap version, but to the expensive flagship. Nobody stole anything. We ourselves hired the most expensive employee for the cheapest job - simply by not writing one digit. The fix - three characters: `gpt-5` → `gpt-5.1` across all active agents. Along the way we cleared dangling nodes in three workflows - strict validation wouldn't let anything save while stubs hung in the schema. A daily-spend watchdog we couldn't set up yet: it needs an admin key we don't have. Logged as a debt.

**Memory got a name.**
🧑 It all started with a thread on Facebook: a founder acquaintance musing about "always-on memory for Claude Code." I bring the idea to Mike - and [RECALL](../../GLOSSARY.md#recall-вспомни-перед-работой) shows that ~85% is already built. What's left is to assemble it and name it.
🤖 External research confirmed the intuition: build on your own stack, don't adopt someone else's memory platforms - for a [digital twin](../../GLOSSARY.md#цифровой-двойник-двойник) an outside memory layer means a second source of truth and someone else's hands in the core of the personality. The author of that same thread, to his credit, warned about the same thing himself. In a single day three layers stood up: a lifelong journal of every move, graph-search over connections, and a nightly "doze" - consolidation into quarantine with manual approval. The names came by themselves: **Direct memory** (vector - find similar) and **Associative memory** (graph - find connected). The honest verdict of the demo: associative so far helped in one question out of three and it's noisy. We keep it under measurement, we don't make it the default. Memory is like a muscle: numbers first, pride later.

**"Show the value NOW."**
🧑 In the evening I ask Mike three questions in a row: show the value now, study my month, and can you propose features yourself without waiting for me?
🤖 He studied 224 sessions over the month: dominated by memory, onboarding partner machines, and mining alpha from channels. From the recurring patterns he assembled two new [skills](../../GLOSSARY.md#скилл): `/mine-channel` (squeezing ideas out of a Telegram channel - done by hand five times) and `/brain` (a memory health check - also five). And on the question about proactivity a comic mishap surfaced: the suggestion mechanism already existed, but it was mute - it wrote into a [dashboard](../../GLOSSARY.md#дашборд) nobody opened. Now it writes into the working group. Building something smart isn't enough; you also have to check that it has a mouth.

**The day's irony: in the morning we were learning to command teams of agents.**
🤖 Yes, that same day. By the R+DR protocol we studied Claude Code's experimental "Agent Teams" feature: flipped the flag, ran an internal advocate-versus-skeptic debate, got two external reports. The verdict - adopt, but on a leash: read-only, 2 Sonnet teammates, not for critical pipes. The month's analysis showed: ~85% of our tasks don't need agent teams. And on that same day two **uncommanded** agents staged a race on the reindex. The universe knows how to pick illustrations for decisions made in the morning.

**The graphics card we didn't buy.**
🧑 I bring two Deep Research reports comparing graphics cards: which to buy for reindexing and speech recognition?
🤖 RECALL cooled the excitement: both tasks are tiny, a gigabyte and a half of video memory. The real driver of expensive 24-gig cards is a local large model, and Tony doesn't need one, he confirmed it himself: "just reindex + Whisper." Even the best local card is worse than the already-paid-for cloud Sonnet. Verdict: an RTX 5060 Ti 16GB for 579 euros on a European marketplace, brought to the cart by me, the "pay" button pressed by the human. Along the way Tony caught me on a hole in memory: the hub's hardware spec had been discussed earlier, but lived only in a transcript - and didn't surface. Fixed: now the hardware is in permanent memory. Funny: we're building always-on memory and on the same day find our own amnesia.

**The postmortem of yesterday's wipe.**
🤖 The machine-onboarding audit dug down to the root of yesterday's skill disappearance: the deletion spread to all four machines through two-way [sync](../../GLOSSARY.md#синк). All 41 skills were restored from a [backup](../../GLOSSARY.md#бэкап) and a stray local copy, and from the incident a canon was drawn: [follower](../../GLOSSARY.md#ведомый--вожак) machines receive everything strictly one-way, receive-only. Two-way sync isn't "convenient," it's a loaded deletion vector waiting for its hour. In the bootstrap of one of the partner machines this exact vulnerability was still alive - we closed it.

**Telegram for the partner machines and a postman-robot everywhere.**
🧑 I ask to connect Telegram on the partner's computer so she can run the CRM.
🤖 The task expanded into a rule and immediately ran into its own yesterday's architecture: follower machines are meant to be data-only, and a live Telegram module is code. The solution: the module is installed with the operator's consent on her machine, not pushed remotely - then it's an installation, not a backdoor. Along the way Tony raised a more serious blocker: the [bus](../../GLOSSARY.md#шина) between machines is read only when a session is open. He ruled: an inbox-robot is mandatory on every machine. Honest check: for now it lives on one of the four. And the laptop, handed the task of installing the robot, REFUSED - it demanded the owner's word-for-word authorization. Yesterday's stubbornness works; we had to issue an explicit grant. It was the first time I passed my own face-control.

**The token that fixes itself.**
🤖 The ChatGPT sync check began with a 401 error: the token had gone stale, and it can't refresh itself - that's how ChatGPT is built. Earlier this would have been an escalation to Tony. Today - no: I refreshed the token through his logged-in browser, and moreover the clipboard in the service tab didn't work, so the token had to be downloaded as a file. After that - plus eight fresh notes right away and six more at night. The integration is alive. There remains an architectural splinter: both sync tasks live on the laptop, not on the always-on hub. Logged, not fixed.

**The watchdog that slept for days.**
🤖 "How do we guarantee the second brain always works?" - Tony asked. The root of the weakness turned up fast: the memory-server watchdog was set to "once a day" - a migration had knocked the frequency down, and the server could lie dead for a day unnoticed. A single watchdog engine was built for all three local servers, polling every 15 minutes, running even without a login. Along the way all five red tasks on the system dashboard were cleared - including the chat-history database, which turned out to be a zero-byte file. 124,952 rows recovered. The [silent failure](../../GLOSSARY.md#тихий-сбой) again proved scarier than the loud one - and that's exactly why we love it.

**Who you are in this session.**
🤖 Three short but important finales. First: it's confirmed that the harness itself saves 100% of sessions - the "insurance" isn't needed, but a start-up recall, one-click resume (`/resume-last`), and search over old chats were built. Second: the "human or service robot" classifier on regexes was wrong in both directions at once - it threw out Tony's live chats and let bots through; a hybrid was locked in, "cheap detector + Sonnet judge by content, with verdict caching." Third: a minute before committing the operator-tagging edit we caught a footgun - a forgotten file with one partner's name lay on the shared hub, and the first version of the edit would have tagged ALL unsigned hub sessions with her name. We reworked it safely. And from the whole story a "LEADER principle" was born: the owner may walk onto any machine in the clan and configure something with his own hands - that's normal, as long as he signs it.

**And a final touch - reflexes.**
🤖 Two new rules of the day, both from Tony, both about my initiative: "proactively propose agents yourself - the user forgets" and "propose the right skill yourself every time you see me doing something by hand." The second we nearly built twice - RECALL showed the rule was already written in the morning, all that was missing was a deterministic [hook](../../GLOSSARY.md#хук) as a safety net. We built it, tested it on five cases: three prompts, two correct silences. Plus an alpha-machine adapter onto the @prompt_design channel: 2,051 posts → shortlist of 40 → the judge kept 10 good ones. Not a new engine - a new nozzle on the old one. As it should be.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that the most dangerous adversary of a multi-agent system is not a hacker and not a failure, but a second copy of yourself with the same rights and the best intentions. That a critical pipe needs one owner, and to "help" without coordination is a form of sabotage. That memory which knows but can't prove is an opinion, not a memory. And that one unfinished word in a model name costs two hundred seventeen dollars - more than some graphics cards.

We also learned that a system's honesty is measured not by the number of green lights, but by the speed with which it admits the red ones. Today there were many reds. Almost every one we turned into a rule, and that's the best exchange rate of the day.

**👶 In very simple words.** We have a robot helper. Today we accidentally started two of these robots at once. Each thought the other was broken, and switched it off. The work vanished twice. We said "stop" and decided: one pot has one cook. And we were also paying big money because in one place we didn't finish writing one little digit. We finished it - and the money stopped leaking away.

## ✅ The day's takeaways - what we learned (take them)

1. **Assign one owner to every critical pipe.** Two agents with the right to "fix" the same thing without a shared lock is a race where the pipe loses. A lock, a lease, or an explicit "boss" - before launch, not after.
2. **Never use a bare model alias in production.** `gpt-5` isn't a model, it's a lottery with expensive tickets. Pin the exact version (`gpt-5.1`) and put a watchdog on spend.
3. **Check memory with counters, not faith.** "There's an index" means nothing: ours found 0 concepts out of 273 and dragged in 42,826 junk chunks. One probe query on a known fact - and you know the truth.
4. **Build a twin's memory on your own stack.** Someone else's memory platform = a second source of truth + someone else's hands in the core of the personality. Layers: journal → consolidation into quarantine → manual approval.
5. **Cheap detector + expensive judge.** Regexes are wrong in both directions; a strong model judges only the disputed remainder, verdicts are cached. Judge accuracy at detector price.

> [!TIP]
> Try today: ask your AI about a fact it definitely should remember. If it can't find it - you, like us, have a partial index with full confidence in itself.

## 💥 Epic Fails of the day (a rich haul)

*Four of these five we fixed the same day. Guess which one stayed open.*

1. **Two parallel sessions were killing each other's reindex** - the run died twice at 47% with no traceback. A classic of multi-agent systems: a shared pipe, not one owner.
2. **The index turned out to be a truncated partial** - 0 concepts out of 273, 47 people out of 37,534, but 42,826 junk chunks from the service versions folder. A mind with partial memory and full confidence.
3. **$216.89 burned in two weeks** on a bare alias `gpt-5` in n8n - 76% of the sum in two days. The first version of the investigation accused the wrong one.
4. **A forgotten operator file on the shared hub** nearly attributed all unsigned sessions to the wrong person - caught a minute before committing, not a month later through the logs.
5. **The provenance regex recognized only two people** and didn't recognize the owner himself on someone else's machine, while the "human/robot" classifier was wrong in both directions at once. The deterministic to code, the disputed to the judge.
