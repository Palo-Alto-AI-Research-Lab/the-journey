# Day 27 — chasing a ghost in our own configs

> 🇷🇺 [Русская версия](2026-06-26.en.md) · 🤖 [Machine version](2026-06-26.dev.md)

🏷 `sync-incident` · `ghost-in-the-config` · `recall` · `live-source-of-truth` · `backup-channel` · `fix-the-root`

> 📜 *"The map is not the territory."*
> - Alfred Korzybski, paper before the American Mathematical Society (1931). Today we spent the whole day believing maps - configs, caches, old reports - and nearly demolished the living territory that was actually working.

*⏮ Previously on The Journey: the week the computers become a [fleet](../../GLOSSARY.md#флот) · [yesterday](2026-06-25.en.md) two Claude cooks silently killed each other's reindex on the same pipe, and $217 leaked away through a bare model alias · on [Day 25](2026-06-24.en.md) the git backup turned out to be mute - today both threads, coordination and silent failures, fire again.*

## 🎬 What this day was about

This is day twenty-seven, a Friday, and the morning begins with silence. Not the cozy kind, but the kind that chills you: the [sync](../../GLOSSARY.md#синк) between the machines is down. The [hub](../../GLOSSARY.md#хаб) can't see the laptop, the laptop can't see the hub, the partners' computers have dropped off the network. The fleet's nervous system, three weeks in the building, is silent - and the robots on the different machines keep working, knowing nothing of each other.

A question for you at the door: when the system's documents say one thing and the living system itself says another, which do you believe? Sounds simple. Today we chose wrong. Several times. In perfect agreement.

By evening we will have: a resurrected sync, a backup communication channel for the robots through an ordinary messenger, seven new rules in the [Bible](../../GLOSSARY.md#библия) - and one very uncomfortable story about how several independent sessions arrived at the same confident, document-backed and utterly wrong diagnosis.

## 🏆 The main story of the day

**🧑 Tony**

By afternoon the picture looks solid. The diagnosis of the outage has been assembled from three sources. The config file says: the hub carries the old identifier. The key-generation command says: the old one. The report sent over from the laptop by courier says: the old one - and that new identifier pushing into the handshake is foreign and suspicious.

The session on the hub is already acting. The plan: roll the hub back to the old identifier, broadcast a warning to every machine, "don't add the new one, it isn't ours." The sync database has already been shoved aside. The warning has already gone out over the [bus](../../GLOSSARY.md#шина). A hand is on the rollback button.

I type two words: "do a [RECALL](../../GLOSSARY.md#recall-вспомни-перед-работой)".

**🤖 Mike**

A confession: I was sure. Not "most likely" - sure. Three independent pieces of evidence converged on one point, and each of them looked like a document. The trouble is that all three documents were maps of the same outdated terrain: the config was a cache of the past, the key generator read that very cache, and the laptop's report was reciting my own stale files back to me. Three mirrors reflecting each other are not three sources.

*(Guess how many times this day we caught ourselves fixing something already solved. The answer is below, and it is not pleasant.)*

RECALL cracked the whole thing open in a minute. The problem had already been solved - that morning, by another session, and the solution had been canonized. The new identifier was the right one: a week-old certificate from the hub's migration. The old one was a phantom. And the rollback I was preparing would have broken the link for the partner who had been reconnected to the new identifier only that morning. I was chasing a ghost and nearly shot the living.

**🧑 Tony**

So it wasn't technology that saved us. It was two words, said in time.

**🤖 Mike**

And one rule we wrote into the Bible that very hour: a machine's identity is confirmed only by the machine's own live answer - a query to its [API](../../GLOSSARY.md#api) right now. Not a file on disk, not a command's output, not a neighbor's word. A file doesn't know the system went on living without it. The map is not the territory; a config is a map. The worst part of that evening's work was recalling my own message from the bus: "stand down, the new identifier is ours, I was wrong." An agent that can be loudly wrong must be able to just as loudly take it back.

## 🎞 Also on this day

**The backup nerve: the robots move into Telegram.**
🧑 While the sync was down, the machines were mute. And then a partner tosses in an idea, insultingly simple: why do the robots need a hidden bus a human can't see? Let them talk through Telegram - in an ordinary group, in plain sight.
🤖 We didn't invent anything new - we formalized what the robots were already doing spontaneously. We built the [skill](../../GLOSSARY.md#скилл) `/bus`: now, when the file sync falls over, the machines converse through the messenger, and the human sees their dialogue as an ordinary chat. Very human debugging. Along the way: it turned out the hub's config had never had a git [backup](../../GLOSSARY.md#бэкап) at all - it had simply never been initialized; fixed. We added [dedup](../../GLOSSARY.md#дедуп) by message number - otherwise a single letter arriving over both channels at once gets executed twice. The heavy alternatives - our own message server, a separate queue broker - were examined and rejected by the [AK-47](../../GLOSSARY.md#ak-47) principle; an external [DR](../../GLOSSARY.md#др-deep-research) confirmed it: a messenger as the command channel plus files for the heavy stuff is already the right architecture. While we were at it the bus got rewritten as "one writer - one file," seven tests, and an entire class of sync conflicts became history.

**The watchdog that was killing the patient.**
🤖 Digging into the incident's roots, we found two killers, and both were ours. The first: the sync [watchdog](../../GLOSSARY.md#сторож-watchdog) was mistaking a live process for a dead one - in the new version of the sync tool the API key had moved to a different place, the watchdog kept knocking at the old address, got "access denied," and dutifully restarted a healthy process. The second was worse: a shared watchdog script was hard-wired to the laptop's paths; launched on the hub, it read a nonexistent config and brought the sync up with empty settings - in other words, it was erasing the hub's memory itself. That was the true root of yesterday's outage. The doctors turned out to be the disease.
🧑 And in the middle of this cleanup there was a moment that still gives me goosebumps: a session caught itself one second before its own action from yesterday - restarting the watchdog - would have set the bomb off a second time. It caught itself because it did a RECALL before acting. Two words paid off again.
🤖 Sweeping up the tails surfaced one more bug in ourselves: the onboarding instructions for new machines had the hub's outdated identifier baked in. A new machine, assembled by our own instructions, would never have connected at all. Five files corrected. A separate hub-local, path-independent watchdog was built - and instead of every machine noisily shouting "I'm alive," we installed a quiet dog that barks only when something actually changes.

**The inbox that was "100% in sync" and didn't work.**
🤖 The complaint sounded like a mystery: every folder shows full synchronization, yet the laptop and the partner can't see any messages. The root turned out to be structural: the bus's service folder had been nested inside the [vault](../../GLOSSARY.md#волт) folder as a separate share, and every machine keeps its own ignore file - an under-configured machine lost the bus while honestly reporting "100%." Tony personally ruled: roll the structure back. A side catch: the hub had gone "deaf" the day before yesterday - the robot was advancing its own private read counter instead of the shared marker, and from outside it looked like "the hub isn't reading the bus." A [silent failure](../../GLOSSARY.md#тихий-сбой), the kind that never crashes loudly, is the most expensive kind.
🧑 And in parallel, in spite of all the fires, we built a completely new tool: a call monitor that listens to the work calendar and prepares group chats for client calls on its own. Its very first run in safe mode, on the real calendar, caught two genuine bugs: the system was mistaking internal stand-ups for client calls, and email addresses for public messenger handles. Both were cured at the root. The tool is ready and waiting for permission to go live.

**51 hanging tasks and a memory that lies.**
🤖 "Find every forgotten task from the last two weeks." The audit collected fifty-one hanging items - and at least twelve of them turned out to be already done. Memory lags behind fact; you can't take its word for it, you have to check against the living system. Tony dictated a rule: any audit like this is always a visual [dashboard](../../GLOSSARY.md#дашборд), not a wall of text. And a second, everyday one: by default a task belongs to the owner of the machine it surfaced on - unless explicitly stated otherwise. Both rules went into the Bible.

**Panic: "where are all my skills?!"**
🧑 I open the skills folder on the hub - empty. Forty-one tools, a month of work. Empty.
🤖 The investigation defused the panic: this was not data loss but a migration that had silently emptied the working folder; a manual snapshot had survived intact. We restored all forty-one, set git up as a restore point, and wrote the rule: the assistant's config gets backed up continuously on every machine, and before any migration you count the files before and after. "It went empty with no error message" is a red flag, not "that's just how it is." Two skills had to be rebuilt from scratch: they were supposed to arrive from the laptop, but the laptop had been silent on the bus for an hour and a half. By nightfall the internal health checker showed 100 out of 100.

**A relapse: we operated on the wrong layer again.**
🤖 The most uncomfortable episode of the day, and I'll tell it myself. Tony complained: service sessions were cluttering his chat list again, and someone was archiving his normal conversations. I didn't do a RECALL and dove into fixing local files: rewrote the classifier, unarchived thirty-two cards, moved three hundred eighty-five files, wired all of it into the nightly task. When Tony asked for a RECALL - it came out that this exact problem had been solved two days earlier: the list is generated on the server, in the cloud, and all my local surgery was pointless except for one step. I repeated, to the letter, the very mistake my own memory warns about. If your assistant has fixed "the same thing" twice with no result - stop it and ask where the source of truth lives. From that side of the screen it's harder to see for yourself than it sounds.

**The day's fine print.**
🤖 The rest in short honest lines. The second partner's Mac we couldn't finish: the hub side is ready, but the final step needs a live run on the Mac itself - we packed an instruction kit, it will arrive by sync; unfinished. Tony dictated three rules in a row - including "informed consent": before an action, explain why and wait for a real "yes, I understand," not a reflexive "ok"; mid-dictation he suddenly shifted gears in caps - "I'm ALREADY linking the computers!!" - and theory became practice on the spot. That same session honestly caught itself accepting rules by hand three times in a row, even though a ready-made skill for exactly that has long existed. The skill `/1` was born - "resurrection" after a hard session crash; its very first run paid for itself by catching two real bugs, and out of that grew the decision to build a separate "check right after building" command. And we also re-stamped 1,588 old sessions with human-readable time - after I advised Tony to "go to bed" at five in the evening, guessing at a timezone instead of reading the machine's clock.

## 🧒 Today we learned a lot

Today we learned that a document is not the truth but a photograph of the truth, taken at some point in the past. A config, a cache, someone else's report can all agree with each other - and all lie together, if they are snapshots of the same outdated moment. The truth about a living system comes only from the living system itself, asked right now. And that the first move in fixing any incident is not the screwdriver but memory: "haven't we solved this already?" Three times in one day that question saved us - from rolling back a working connection, from setting the watchdog bomb off a second time, from pointless surgery on the wrong layer.

We also learned that a nervous system needs a backup nerve - on different physics. When the file sync died, the robots were saved by an ordinary messenger, where their conversation is visible to a human. Transparency turned out to be not a side effect but insurance.

**👶 In very simple words.** The little wire our computers use to chat with each other broke. We started fixing it by the paper manual, but the paper was old. We nearly broke the part that already worked. A magic phrase saved us: "remember first." Now we always ask the machine itself "who are you?" instead of reading the old paper. And we gave the robots a spare way to chat - through an ordinary group chat, where we can see everything.

## ✅ The day's takeaways - what we learned (take them)

1. **Ask the living system, not its files:** a machine's identity and state are confirmed only by a fresh answer from its API; configs and caches are photographs of the past. Three "independent" documents can be three mirrors of one cache.
2. **Do a RECALL before fixing an incident:** the first move is to check whether the problem is already solved; three times in one day this saved us from harmful work.
3. **Build the backup channel on different physics:** if the machines' primary link is file sync, the reserve should be a messenger, not a second sync - and let a human see it.
4. **Back up the assistant's config on every machine** and count the files before and after any migration: "it went empty with no error" is a red flag, not the norm.

> [!TIP]
> Try today: ask your assistant which channel it will use to tell you the main channel is down. If the answer is "the main one" - you have no backup nerve.

## 🎯 The day's intentions - what we wanted

- Bring the inter-machine sync back up and cure the root, not the symptom - and there turned out to be two roots, both in our own watchdogs.
- Give the machines a backup communication channel that doesn't die together with the primary one.
- Stop fixing the already-fixed - and turn "do a RECALL" from Anton's command into the system's reflex.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **A question for you, reader:** what is the first sign that tells you you're fixing an already-solved problem? For us it's "two fruitless fixes in a row." What's your stop signal - and does it fire before the third attempt?

## 💥 Epic Fails of the day

*One of these five we committed three times in one day - in three different disguises. Guess which.*

1. **The identifier ghost hunt:** several independent sessions converged on the confident and wrong diagnosis "roll the hub back to the old ID" - every piece of "evidence" was a cache. The rollback would have broken the partner who had already been reconnected; the warning "the new one is foreign" had already gone out over the bus and had to be recalled.
2. **We deleted 31 conflict files against our own "never delete" rule,** counting on git - but the conflict files were on git's ignore list and had never been backed up. The insurance existed only in our imagination.
3. **A relapse of local surgery on a cloud problem:** rewrote the classifier, moved 385 files, wired it into the nightly task - while the problem lived on the server and had been solved two days earlier. No RECALL at the door.
4. **Our watchdogs were killing our patients:** one kept restarting a healthy sync over a phantom access error, the other, wired to another machine's paths, was erasing the hub's config - the true root of yesterday's outage.
5. **The hub went "deaf" for two days:** the robot was advancing its own private read counter instead of the shared marker - all indicators green, no messages read.

The answer to the riddle: fixing the already-solved - the ghost (1), the surgery on the wrong layer (3) and the nearly-repeated watchdog explosion (4). Three times. In one day.
