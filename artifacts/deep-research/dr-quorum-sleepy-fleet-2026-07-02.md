# Quorum Design for a Sleeping Personal Fleet

## Executive Summary

The hard constraint is not philosophical, it is mathematical: a strict two-vote rule cannot be made *universally* live in an `N=4` fleet with only one always-on machine if the other three may all be asleep. Sleepy-consensus research exists precisely because classical fixed-participant protocols break under sporadic participation, and the foundational result is that consensus in this model depends on who is actually online now, not on the nominal fleet size. Academic sleepy-consensus work says two things that matter directly here. The most relevant research synthesis for your use case is that quorum-based sleepy protocols extend BFT from **static quorum size to dynamic quorum size according to current participation level**, but this usually comes with caveats. The survey work around Goldfish, RLMD-GHOST, and related sleepy protocols notes that some quorum-based designs support dynamic participation only if there are periods of stable participation for liveness, and that there is a persistent trade-off between dynamic participation tolerance and asynchrony resilience. ## Contradictions & Debates

The biggest technical contradiction is that **dynamic availability** and **strong asynchrony resilience** pull in opposite directions. Research on generalized sleepy models shows that some dynamically available quorum protocols become fragile under even temporary asynchrony, while more reorg-resilient designs restrict dynamic participation harder. EthereumŌĆÖs own fast-finality research is now explicitly grappling with ŌĆ£accountable livenessŌĆØ and how to resume finalizing after trouble, which is an admission that faster finality does not make the liveness problem disappear. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn26view0Ņłéturn23view3Ņłü

EthereumŌĆÖs inactivity leak is often praised as a brilliant liveness rescue, and in one sense it is. But the official explanation also concedes the brutal trade-off: under severe long-lived partitions, the chain may end up preferring availability over consistency and could permanently split. That is acceptable for a global public chain because weeks-long catastrophic partitions are rarer than day-to-day validator churn. It is *not* the right mental model for a personal fleet: you should borrow the *explicitness* of the rescue, not the penalty mechanism or the willingness to let long partitions redefine truth. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view1Ņłü

There is also a governance debate around quorum itself. DAO research in the last year argues that broader participation has capacity limits, and that as proposal flow rises, effective governance concentrates in a smaller set of highly active participants. Another 2026 paper finds that quorum and voting-power thresholds may actually worsen decentralization outcomes by suppressing inclusive participation. That does not mean ŌĆ£never use quorum.ŌĆØ It means high quorums are notorious for killing proposals through apathy and monitoring overload rather than improving judgment quality. The alternative camp says: stop using ex ante quorum for everything. Apache-style lazy consensus treats silence as consent after an objection window, and optimistic rollups let proposals execute before finality, guarded by a challenge period. These approaches maximize liveness, but only if the proposal class is narrow, reversibility exists, and the challenge window is credible. ## Best Practices

The first best practice is to make **availability explicit**. Add a tiny `presence` or `heartbeat` event to the JSONL bus and define an **awake lease**: a machine is ŌĆ£awake nowŌĆØ only if the hub has seen a fresh signed event from it within a short lease window. This is the practical substitute for AlgorandŌĆÖs online participation keys and for dynamic-quorum sleepy protocols: if a machine is asleep, it is not part of the current committee. Do not wait eight or twenty-four hours for a committee member who is not in the current committee. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view3Ņłéturn26view0Ņłü

The second best practice is to freeze the committee **at proposal snapshot time**, not at deadline time. OpenZeppelin governance docs make the same design choice for quorum checkpoints so outcome rules cannot be altered retroactively. Your fleet should do the same: once the hub opens round `r`, the eligible committee for that round is fixed. Late wake-ups can vote in round `r+1`, or participate in the challenge or verify window, but they do not get to change the quorum requirement of the current round. That single choice prevents moving-goalpost deadlocks and makes logs interpretable by a non-technical owner. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28view0Ņłü

The third best practice is to use **role constraints instead of vote inflation**. Do not let the leader count as `N` votes. Require that at least one vote in a strict certificate comes from the anchor set, but keep every machineŌĆÖs vote weight at `1`. AmazonŌĆÖs leader-election guidance is useful here: leaders simplify control flow, but they also increase blast radius and single-point dependence. A visibly logged `degraded_commit_by_leader` is honest and debuggable; ŌĆ£leader counts as twoŌĆØ is neither. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn31view0Ņłü

The fourth best practice is to separate **soft agreement** from **hard finality**. Borrow CasperŌĆÖs checkpoint intuition, not its machinery. In your fleet: `accepted` means the acceptance certificate exists; `committed` means the hub executed and published the certificate; `finalized` means the verify or challenge window passed cleanly. That mirrors the way Ethereum distinguishes justification from finalization and the way optimistic systems distinguish execution from settled correctness. It also gives you a place to keep your false-VERIFY defenses without putting all of that weight on the liveness-critical accept path. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view2Ņłéturn19view6Ņłéturn23view2Ņłü

The fifth best practice is to make every liveness rescue **costly in attention**, not in stake. EthereumŌĆÖs inactivity leak restores liveness by penalizing inactive validators; that is appropriate for a public chain, not a home AI fleet. Your analog should be a loudly visible state transition: `strict_quorum_unreachable`, `degraded_commit`, `challenge_open`, `human_required`. Rescue should never look like normal success in the logs. If degraded paths are too convenient, the system will silently route work around quorum so often that the quorum becomes theater. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn19view1Ņłéturn21view2Ņłü

The sixth best practice is to copy validator **slashing-protection state**, not validator economics. Prysm and Cosmos KMS both maintain tiny durable state files that record the highest signed slot, epoch, height, round, or step, and refuse regressions. That is precisely what your fleet should do for `accept`, `commit`, and `verify`: every node needs a persisted signing watermark, and restores or migrations must import that watermark before the node is allowed to vote again. This protects against replayed accepts after rollback, stale backups, duplicate live instances, and some clock-skew edge cases. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn30view1Ņłéturn30view0Ņłéturn30view2Ņłü

The seventh best practice is intentional **software diversity in the verifier lane**. EthereumŌĆÖs own operational guidance points to client diversity as a live safety priority because a bug in a dominant client can drag participation below finality thresholds. In a trusted personal fleet, you do not need five independent consensus clients, but you do want one or two verification paths that are not the same optimistic agent build as the proposer. Otherwise a shared bug can produce quorum agreement on the same wrong answer. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn23view0Ņłéturn23view1Ņłü

## Failure Modes

The primary failure mode is **quorum inflation**: raising the rule from one vote to two votes while still implicitly waiting on the whole fleet. Sleepy-consensus literature and TendermintŌĆÖs own liveness properties both say this is a stall factory. If the engine treats nominal membership as available membership, sleeping honest nodes behave like withheld votes, and honest absence becomes indistinguishable from an adversarial halt. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn16view0Ņłéturn12search0Ņłü

A close cousin is the **moving denominator** failure. If the hub decides that quorum is ŌĆ£two of whoever looks online at the deadline,ŌĆØ then newly woken or newly missing machines can change the victory condition while the proposal is in flight. Governance systems explicitly avoid this by checkpointing quorum and voting power at snapshot time. Your fleet needs the same discipline because it makes dead proposals diagnosable and prevents operators from asking, ŌĆ£Why did the rule change after the proposal was already open?ŌĆØ ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28view0Ņłü

Then there is **double-vote by restore or failover accident**. EthereumŌĆÖs validator docs are blunt: never run the same validator key on two machines at once, export and import slashing-protection history when moving, and accept some downtime rather than chasing perfect uptime with sloppy failover. Cosmos KMS says the same thing in different words: double-sign protection depends on a shared state file, and multiple instances without that shared state remove the protection. Your fleet has the same problem if a restored laptop can replay or reissue `accept` or `verify` from an older local view. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn30view2Ņłéturn30view1Ņłéturn30view0Ņłü

A separate but related issue is **identity drift**. Distributed systems often bind durable node identity to local persistent state; when that state is reset, the system effectively sees a new node. RabbitMQŌĆÖs clustering docs, for example, warn that resetting a node deletes its data and alters its internal identity. That is the right mental model for your foreign-ID-style identity regeneration incidents: a regenerated identity must be quarantined as a new observer until the old identity is explicitly retired or rebound by a durable registry. Never let a node silently reappear under a fresh identity and inherit voting power automatically. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn9search19Ņłü

Another routine killer is **clock misuse on wake**. AmazonŌĆÖs leader-election guidance is explicit that distributed systems should not rely on synchronized wall-clock time for coordination, and that leases must be based on local elapsed duration, with checks before side effects. Your fleet should therefore order protocol events by `(term, round, step, sequence)` and treat timestamps as audit metadata for humans, not as the source of truth for causal ordering. Wake-from-sleep clock jumps are annoying; they become consensus failures only if you let them define ordering. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn31view0Ņłü

The last recurring failure mode is **monoculture and correlated bugs**. EthereumŌĆÖs 2023 non-finalization events and PrysmŌĆÖs 2025 participation incident both underline the same point: a shared software bug can depress participation enough to threaten finality even when no one is malicious. ## Strategic Recommendations

The design recommendation is an **anchored awake-committee protocol**. It uses your existing file-sync bus and JSONL logs, adds only a tiny presence lease and a per-node signing watermark, and turns the hub into an explicit liveness engine rather than a hidden super-voter. The synthesis comes from sleepy-consensus dynamic committees, Tendermint round structure, optimistic challenge windows, governance snapshotting, and validator slashing-protection practice. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn26view0Ņłéturn19view0Ņłéturn19view6Ņłéturn28view0Ņłéturn30view1Ņłü

Use these common rules across both fleet sizes:

| Item | Recommended rule |
|---|---|
| Presence | Every awake machine appends a signed `presence` event at wake and every 15ŌĆō30 minutes while awake. |
| Awake committee | For proposal `p`, the leader freezes `committee(p)` from machines with an unexpired presence lease at snapshot time. |
| Strict tier-1 certificate | Two `accept` votes from distinct non-owner machines, with **at least one anchor vote**. |
| Anchor set | `N=4`: the always-on hub. `N=10`: primary hub + second always-on hub. |
| No moving quorum | Late wake-ups do not change current-round quorum; they join the next round or the challenge/verify lane. |
| No leader weight inflation | Leader counts as one vote. Any leader-only rescue must be logged as a distinct degraded path. |
| Finality states | `accepted` ŌåÆ `committed` ŌåÆ `finalized`, with reopen only by explicit superseding proposal. |
| Replay defense | Each node persists a vote watermark and refuses conflicting or regressive signatures after restore or migration. |

For `N=4` with one always-on hub and three sleeping machines, the honest answer is that strict two-vote tier-1 is only reachable **when at least one sleeper is awake at snapshot time**. Therefore the protocol must say that out loud instead of waiting on fantasy quorum. The recommended per-tier rules are these:

| Tier | `N=4` exact rule |
|---|---|
| Tier-0 | Leader proposes; if no `counter` from any awake peer within **60 minutes**, leader writes `commit_soft`. Challenge/verify window stays open for **24 hours**. |
| Tier-1 strict | If at least one sleeper is awake at snapshot: require `leader + 1 sleeper` **or** `2 sleepers`; wait **60 minutes** for the certificate. |
| Tier-1 degraded | If no sleeper is awake at snapshot: strict quorum is declared unreachable immediately. For **reversible** actions only, leader may write `tentative_commit` after **60 minutes**, with a **24-hour** challenge window. Any `counter` or failed `verify` reopens. |
| Tier-1 irreversible | If no sleeper is awake at snapshot, **do not auto-commit**. Page or queue for human; hard human floor at **24 hours**. |
| Tier-2 | Require explicit `human_approve` before commit. After approval, leader commits; keep at least one independent verify in the post-commit lane. |

For `N=10` with two always-on machines and eight sleepers, you can make tier-1 genuinely reachable without cheating, because the two-anchor baseline exists even if every sleeper is down. The recommended rules are these:

| Tier | `N=10` exact rule |
|---|---|
| Tier-0 | Primary hub proposes; if no `counter` from secondary hub or any awake reviewer within **60 minutes**, primary hub writes `commit_soft`. Challenge/verify window remains **24 hours**. |
| Tier-1 strict | Require `primary hub + secondary hub` **or** `primary hub + any awake reviewer`; if the primary is failed and standby lease transfer occurred cleanly, allow `secondary hub + any awake reviewer`. Standard wait is **60 minutes**. |
| Tier-1 degraded | Only if the secondary hub is itself unavailable. For reversible actions, allow `tentative_commit` after **8 hours** if no second vote arrives and no objections exist; keep the challenge window open through **24 hours**. |
| Tier-1 irreversible | If secondary hub is unavailable and no second reviewer appears, escalate to human no later than **24 hours**. |
| Tier-2 | Require `human_approve + primary hub + secondary hub`. That buys materially more safety at almost no liveness cost because both anchors are always on. |

The finality marker should be small and boring. After any strict tier-1 certificate, the hub writes a `commit` event containing the proposal hash, the acceptance certificate, and a post-state digest. The decision is then **committed but not finalized**. It becomes **finalized** when either one independent verifier confirms the post-state and no challenge exists, or the full **24-hour** challenge window closes quietly for low-risk work. Once finalized, it is never reopened in place; it can only be superseded by a new proposal that references the old decision ID. That is the smallest useful analog of blockchain checkpoint finality. On weighted or role quorums, the recommendation is simple: **use anchor requirements, not giant anchor weights**. If you want a leader override, make it a separate protocol state with different logging, different alerts, and a narrower allow-list. This also keeps the system repairable: the owner can grep for `tentative_commit` and know immediately that the degraded path was used. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn31view0Ņłéturn20view0Ņłü

## Action Plan

First, add two tiny protocol primitives and nothing else: a signed `presence` event and a persisted `vote_watermark` file per machine. The watermark stores the highest `(term, round, step, proposal_hash)` that the machine has signed, and the hub also keeps a copy. On restore or migration, the node must import that watermark before it can vote. This is the single highest-leverage reliability step because it blocks replay, duplicate processes, and stale-backup ghosts before they become consensus incidents. Second, introduce the new decision states in the JSONL engine: `propose`, `counter`, `accept`, `commit_soft`, `tentative_commit`, `finalize`, `supersede`, and `human_approve`. Fifth, test the failure modes before rollout.
